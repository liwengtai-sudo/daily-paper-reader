## Abstract 

Time-series anomaly detection (TSAD) is a crit-ical component in monitoring complex systems, yet modern deep learning–based detectors are of-ten highly sensitive to localized input corrup-tions and structured noise. We propose ARTA 

(Adversarially Robust multivariate Time-series 

Anomaly detection via joint information retention ), a joint training framework that improves detec-tor robustness through a principled min-max opti-mization objective. ARTA comprises an anomaly detector and a sparsity-constrained mask genera-tor that are trained simultaneously. The genera-tor identifies minimal, task-relevant temporal per-turbations that maximally increase the detector’s anomaly score, while the detector is optimized to remain stable under these structured perturba-tions. The resulting masks characterize the detec-tor’s sensitivity to adversarial temporal corruptions and can serve as explanatory signals for the de-tector’s decisions. This adversarial training strat-egy exposes brittle decision pathways and encour-ages the detector to rely on distributed and sta-ble temporal patterns rather than spurious localized artifacts. We conduct extensive experiments on the TSB-AD benchmark, demonstrating that ARTA consistently improves anomaly detection perfor-mance across diverse datasets and exhibits signif-icantly more graceful degradation under increasing noise levels compared to state-of-the-art baselines. 

## 1 Introduction 

Multivariate time-series anomaly detection (TSAD) is the foundation of safety-critical monitoring systems in domains such as transportation [Hojjati et al. , 2023] and healthcare. In these applications, anomaly scores are not merely used to rank events, but directly drive alarms, mitigation strate-gies, and human intervention. Accordingly, TSAD systems must exhibit score stability: small, localized perturbations in the input should not induce large changes in anomaly scores unless the underlying system dynamics have meaningfully changed. Recent evidence, however, indicates that this re-quirement is rarely met. Despite their architectural complex-ity, state-of-the-art deep TSAD models evaluated on modern benchmarks often yield marginal improvements over simple baselines while exhibiting severe fragility [Kim et al. , 2022]. In real deployments, commonplace phenomena such as tran-sient noise, sensor jitter, or partial channel failures can trigger sharp spikes in anomaly scores even under nominal operating conditions, fundamentally undermining their reliability. This fragility stems from a fundamental misalignment between training objectives and deployment requirements [Wang et al. , 2025a]. Most TSAD methods are optimized to minimize point-wise reconstruction or prediction errors un-der implicit i.i.d. train–test assumptions. Such objectives in-centivize the memorization of local, high-frequency artifacts rather than the learning of a compact, noise-robust represen-tation of normal system behavior. Consequently, ubiquitous deployment noise is frequently misinterpreted as anomalous [Zhang et al. , 2024]. The problem is further amplified by window-based, segment-level detection pipelines: while in-tended to capture temporal dependencies, they allow small, localized perturbations to propagate through an entire seg-ment’s representation. This induces a strong dependence of anomaly scores on the perturbation’s position within the slid-ing window, leading to inconsistent and unstable detections across otherwise identical events [Wang et al. , 2025b]. Although recent work explores data augmentation [Luo et al. , 2023], corruption-aware training [Xing et al. , 2025], and robust losses [Kim et al. , 2025], these approaches mainly improve average-case performance and do not explicitly ad-dress worst-case, localized temporal perturbations. They lack mechanisms to enforce score consistency under structured, correlated noise, which is the dominant failure mode in real deployments. Time-series corruptions span diffuse noise, cor-related disturbances, and sparse local failures; while exist-ing methods handle the first, they remain brittle to the other regimes [Gu et al. , 2025]. We argue that robustness in TSAD should be induced directly through the training objective by explicitly penalizing localized temporal sensitivity. This per-spective motivates approaches that embed robustness into the learning process itself, rather than treating it as post hoc ad-ditions. Motivated by this principle, we propose ARTA, a joint training framework that explicitly targets score stability un-der localized perturbations. ARTA couples an anomaly de-tector with a sparsity-constrained temporal mask generator in

an adversarial game: the generator learns to identify minimal temporal regions whose perturbation maximally disrupts the detector’s anomaly score. Crucially, sparsity constraints force these perturbations to remain compact and localized, closely mirroring realistic sensor failures, such as dropouts, spikes, saturation, or miscalibration, which are structured and tem-porally correlated. Despite its conceptual simplicity, this paradigm remains largely unexplored in TSAD. Unlike prior masking-based heuristics [Lee et al. , 2024], the generator is not a passive explanation module but an active component that regularizes the detector’s decision boundary during training. To the best of our knowledge, ARTA is the first framework to jointly leverage adversarial masking and information retention to ex-plicitly regularize temporal robustness in TSAD. We evalu-ate ARTA on diverse datasets from the TSB-AD benchmark, utilizing metrics that assess both detection accuracy and ro-bustness to structured corruptions. Our results demonstrate consistent improvements in anomaly detection performance alongside substantial gains in score stability under localized perturbations. Our main contributions are summarized as: 1. We argue that instability to localized temporal corrup-tions is a fundamental limitation of current paradigms and demonstrate that resolving it is a key driver for su-perior performance on modern benchmarks. 2. We introduce ARTA, a joint adversarial framework that internalizes robustness by training detectors against sparsity-constrained worst-case temporal perturbations. 3. Through extensive evaluation on modern benchmarks, we show that ARTA achieves a superior trade-off be-tween detection accuracy, robustness, and interpretabil-ity compared to both classical TSAD models and mod-ern deep baselines. 

## 2 Related Works 

Time-series anomaly detection has recently undergone a ma-jor methodological reassessment. For many years, apparent progress in the field was driven by increasingly complex deep learning architectures, ranging from attention-based models to graph neural networks, yet subsequent analyses revealed that much of this progress was illusory [Wu and Keogh, 2022]. A central issue was the widespread use of window-level evaluation schemes, such as Point Adjustment (PA), which substantially inflated performance by tolerating im-precise temporal localization. Under these protocols, mod-els could achieve high scores even when failing to identify anomalies at individual timestamps, obscuring fundamental weaknesses in point-wise detection [Kim et al. , 2022]. This evaluation crisis motivated the development of stricter benchmarks, most notably TSB-AD [Liu et al. , 2024]. Re-sults on TSB-AD demonstrate that many previously reported SOTA methods degrade sharply when evaluated under refined datasets and strict protocols. Consequently, recent research has shifted away from architectural novelty alone toward methods that emphasize robustness and precise point-wise discrimination under realistic noise conditions. In response to these stricter standards, two dominant paradigms emerged in the past year. The first is the rise of lightweight pre-trained or foundation models for TSAD. Among these, TSPulse [Ekambaram et al. , 2025] represents the current state of the art, favoring efficiency over scale. Unlike large language model–based approaches such as MOMENT [Goswami et al. , 2024] or Chronos [Ansari et al. , 2024], TSPulse employs an ultra-compact architecture with approximately one mil-lion parameters and learns through masked reconstruction in both time and frequency domains. A multi-head triangulation mechanism combines reconstruction and forecasting devia-tions to produce anomaly scores, yielding strong performance on the TSB-AD leaderboard and surpassing baselines by a substantial margin. Nevertheless, as a reconstruction-driven method, TSPulse remains vulnerable to the identity-mapping problem: high-capacity decoders can faithfully reconstruct stochastic or high-frequency noise, blurring the distinction between benign perturbations and true point-level anomalies. A second line of work reframes TSAD through the lens of dynamical systems. FREE [yon Jhin and Park, 2026] departs from conventional reconstruction or forecasting errors and in-stead models anomaly emergence as the accumulation of sys-tem stress. By extracting sensitivity and uncertainty-based stress signals and integrating them through a fold-bifurcation ordinary differential equation, it explicitly targets point-wise anomaly detection as a dynamical tipping phenomenon. This formulation avoids window-level aggregation and achieves strong point-wise performance. However, FREE relies on calibrated thresholds derived from normal-system dynamics rather than learning an explicit discriminative boundary, leav-ing it sensitive to noise that mimic early stress signals. Despite these advancements, a fundamental limitation per-sists across the current TSAD landscape: robust point-wise detection under noisy conditions remains unresolved. Many contemporary methods implicitly retreat from strict point-level decision-making, instead relying on smoothing, block-level reporting, or post-hoc aggregation to suppress false pos-itives. Reconstruction-based models tend to absorb noise into their learned representations, while dynamical approaches model stability without actively rejecting adversarial pertur-bations [Gu et al. , 2025]. As a result, reliable discrimination between stochastic noise and structurally meaningful anoma-lies at individual timestamps remains an open challenge, even among leading methods on TSB-AD. To address this gap, we propose an approach that integrates adversarial training directly into point-wise TSAD. By ex-posing the model to adversarial perturbations during train-ing and coupling this with a mask-based architecture, we ex-plicitly enforce a robust decision boundary between noise and anomalies at the finest temporal resolution. This de-sign directly targets the noise-mixing failure modes revealed by strict point-wise benchmarks and complements recent ad-vances by adding principled robustness rather than additional architectural complexity.

3.2 Mask Generator and Perturbation Model 

The mask generator Gϕ takes the input time-series and pro-duces a real-valued temporal mask 

M = Gϕ(X), M ∈ [0 , 1] T (4) where T denotes the size of the mask that identifies influential time stamps for the detector’s anomaly score. We restrict the mask to the temporal dimension to encourage sensor-agnostic temporal robustness and avoid trivial feature-wise suppres-sion. The mask is broadcast across feature dimensions and ap-plied to the input using a baseline-aware perturbation model: 

˜X = X ⊙ M + (1 − M ) ⊙ B (5) where B is a baseline signal (e.g., per-sensor mean or zero), and ⊙ denotes element-wise multiplication. This formulation avoids scale artifacts and distribution shifts associated with hard masking. In our implementation, the baseline B is cho-sen as the per-sensor temporal mean computed within each input sequence, without using future context, resulting in a scale-preserving and in-distribution perturbation.This choice ensures that masked regions are replaced with a statistically neutral reference signal, preventing the introduction of arti-ficial discontinuities that could lead to trivial adversarial so-lutions. Alternative baseline configurations are explored in Appendix 12. Since the generator operates exclusively on normal training data, it learns to identify model-sensitive tem-poral regions that expose the detector’s vulnerabilities, rather than localizing true anomalous patterns. While conventional adversarial training typically injects diffuse, unstructured noise, our framework instead constructs temporally structured, sparsity-constrained perturbations that remain domain-aligned and closely reflect real-world failure modes. Diffuse i.i.d. noise is deliberately excluded from the training objective: such noise is already well handled by stan-dard data augmentation, smoothing, and robust optimization techniques, and it is not the dominant source of catastrophic anomaly score instability. Our focus is therefore on the more challenging failure regime of localized, structured perturba-tions that disrupt short-term dynamics and induce dispropor-tionate changes in anomaly scores. Additional mathematical proofs are provided in Appendix A to offer deeper insight into the theoretical foundations of ARTA’s robustness. 

3.3 Generator Objective 

The generator is trained to identify minimal temporal regions that are sufficient to increase the detector’s anomaly score. Its objective balances two competing goals: (i) maximizing the anomaly score of the perturbed input, and (ii) enforcing sparsity of the mask. The generator loss is defined as: 

LG(ϕ) = −AD ( ˜X) + λ∥M ∥1 (6) where λ > 0 controls the sparsity strength. The first term encourages the generator to find task-relevant perturbations that increase the detector’s anomaly score ( e.g. reconstruction error in autoencoders), while the ℓ1 regularization promotes compact and localized masks. 

3.4 Detector Objective 

The detector is trained to minimize the anomaly score for both clean and masked signals, encouraging invariance to generator-induced perturbations. The detector loss is defined as: 

LD (θ) = AD (X) + γA D ( ˜X), (7) where γ ≥ 0 controls the emphasis on stability. Note that setting γ = 0 eliminates the loss component responsible for encouraging robustness of the detector with respect to the generator-induced perturbations. Minimizing LD encourages the detector to rely on dis-tributed temporal features rather than a small set of highly sensitive time steps.

## 4 Experiments 

In this section, we evaluate ARTA through a series of ex-periments on the rigorous TSB-AD benchmark [Liu et al. ,2024]. TSB-AD comprises multiple datasets carefully cu-rated to mitigate the evaluation pitfalls and flawed labels com-mon in legacy TSAD benchmarks, ensuring a standardized and high-fidelity assessment. In all experiments, we utilize an LSTM-based autoencoder as the detector, chosen for its inherent inductive bias toward temporal continuity, and an LSTM as the generator. Additional implementation details and an empirical validation of this backbone choice against alternative architectures are provided in Appendix K and I. 

4.1 Comparison with State-of-the-Art 

We first compare ARTA with several state-of-the-art anomaly detection methods (see Appendix C for details). To guaran-tee the integrity of our benchmarking, all baselines were sub-jected to rigorous hyperparameter optimization using the pro-tocols specified in their original implementations. For eval-uation, we use VUS-PR, which is well-suited for time-series anomalies that span multiple consecutive time steps. Stan-dard point-based metrics, such as Precision, Recall, F-score, or AUC-ROC/AUC-PR, often fail to capture the temporal ex-tent of anomalies and are sensitive to noise or small mis-alignments between predicted and true ranges. VUS-PR [Pa-parrizos et al. , 2022] addresses these limitations by providing a threshold-independent, parameter-free assessment. It con-structs a 3D surface over all thresholds and buffer sizes and computes its volume, offering a holistic measure of detec-tion performance for both point- and range-based anomalies. We report performance using other evaluation metrics in Ap-pendix H. Table 1 summarizes the performance of ARTA in comparison with state-of-the-art methods. Our approach consistently achieves high VUS-PR scores across the bench-mark, demonstrating its ability to accurately detect tempo-rally extended anomalies across diverse datasets, which vary in anomaly types, durations, and occurrence ratios. We ob-serve that ARTA tops the performance chart in 7 out of 10 datasets and comes second in the remaining three. Even for the datasets where it ranks second, our subsequent experi-ments in Section 4.2 show potential improvements in robust-ness. These results can be attributed to the training proce-dure of ARTA. The adversarial training encourages the detec-tor to remain stable against mask-guided perturbations, driv-ing the model to focus on distributed temporal patterns rather than isolated point values. This enhances robustness to non-anomalous fluctuations and noise, allowing the model to re-liably distinguish true anomalies during inference. Conse-quently, ARTA captures complex, distributed temporal fea-tures that are critical for accurate and robust anomaly detec-tion across diverse datasets. The following sections further analyze complementary ablations and anomaly scoring strate-gies to isolate the contributions of individual components.

In this regime, the mask generator primarily serves as a training-time regularizer: by enforcing invariance to structured perturba-tions, it enables reconstruction residuals to become a reliable indicator of normality. Since the generator is trained only on normal data and primarily targets vulnerable regions of the learned manifold, its contribution at inference is limited. An exception arises on the OPP dataset, where the mask-weighted score substantially outperforms pure reconstruction. The mask-weighted score functions as a learned attention mechanism, amplifying decision-critical de-viations that reconstruction alone may suppress. This sug-gests that for datasets with manifold-aligned or highly lo-calized anomalies, incorporating generator-informed scoring provides essential contrastive information beyond what re-construction can capture. 

4.4 Interpretation of Adversarial Masks 

A central component of ARTA is the adversarial generator. While the generator’s masks are visually interpretable, it is important to clarify what they explain. Although the gener-ator produces temporal masks, these masks should be inter-preted as explanations of the detector’s sensitivity rather than precise localizations of ground-truth anomalies. The gener-ator is trained adversarially to identify worst-case perturba-tions that maximize the detector’s anomaly response, which need not coincide with the semantic or annotated extent of anomalies. As a result, the learned masks highlight regions that are most influential for the detector’s decision, rather than regions that are intrinsically anomalous. Results of Table 2 empirically support this distinction by showing that including the generator output in anomaly scoring leads to substantial performance degradation compared to the standard detector score, confirming that the adversarial masks are not suitable as standalone anomaly detectors or localization signals. We therefore use the generator output exclusively for post-hoc in-terpretability analysis. Figure 3 presents qualitative examples illustrating the relationship between the input time-series and the corresponding masks. As seen in this figure, the masks consistently focus on sharp transitions or localized temporal regions that strongly influence the detector, even when these regions cover only a subset of the annotated anomaly win-dow. This behavior is consistent with the adversarial objec-tive, which emphasizes sensitivity rather than semantic com-pleteness. These results indicate that the adversarial masks learned by ARTA provide model-faithful explanations, offer-ing insight into where and why the detector reacts strongly, without being conflated with the scoring mechanisms. 

## 5 Conclusion 

We introduced ARTA, an adversarial-robust framework for TSAD that jointly trains a detector and a sparsity-constrained mask generator in an adversarial formulation. By explicitly exposing the detector to worst-case, structured temporal per-turbations during training, ARTA promotes reliance on sta-ble, task-relevant temporal patterns rather than brittle point-wise cues. Extensive evaluations on state-of-the-art bench-marks show that ARTA consistently outperforms state-of-the-art baselines and exhibits significantly improved robustness to input noise. Ablation studies further confirm the complemen-tary roles of adversarial interaction, sparsity regularization, and mask-guided perturbations in driving both performance and robustness gains. We hope this work serves as a bench-mark for the next generation of TSAD methods, encouraging the field to move beyond unreliable evaluation metrics and to place greater emphasis on robustness in algorithm design. A.3 Conceptual Relation to Information Bottleneck Principle 

The Information Bottleneck (IB) principle by Tishby et al. Tishby et al. [1999] is a conceptual framework which could be used to understand how a model extracts the relevant pattern of data. It suggests that the goal of any learning process is to balance the tradeoff between representation compression and prediction accuracy. The IB principle is formulated as an optimization problem using Mutual Information I between the input X and representation Z. We want to find a representation Z that minimizes the following objective function: 

LIB = I(X; Z) − βI (Z; Y ) (13) where I(X; Z) is the information Z captures about the input X, I(Z; Y ) is the information Z captures about the target task Y . We want to minimize I(X; Z) to achieve compression while maximizing I(Z; Y ) to achieve a good performance on solving the task Y . β is a Lagrange multiplier that controls the trade-off. A high β prioritizes accuracy, while a low β prioritizes compression. Although ARTA does not minimize the formal IB objective, it adopts a similar philosophy: the generator’s sparsity constraint enforces a bottleneck that sheds irrelevant temporal data, while the adversarial objective ensures that the remaining information is highly informative regarding the detector’s decision-making process. Thus, the mask identifies the minimal subset of X that preserves the information necessary to influence the anomaly score. Representation Compression via Generator Sparsity: In the IB formulation, representational compression is quantified via the mutual information I(X; Z). In contrast, ARTA constructs purturbed inputs which can be thought of as representations through a deterministic masking operation 

Z = ˜X = X ⊙ M + (1 − M ) ⊙ B, 

where M ∈ [0 , 1] T is a learned temporal mask and B is a baseline signal. Rather than directly estimating mutual information, which is challenging in high-dimensional continuous settings, we interpret the sparsity of M as a structural constraint on the effective information capacity of Z.Specifically, restricting the number of time steps retained by the mask limits the degrees of freedom through which informa-tion from X can influence the detector. Under mild regularity assumptions on the input process, the total information content of the masked representation is upper-bounded (up to dataset-dependent constants) by the number of temporally active compo-nents selected by M . Thus, sparsity serves as a deterministic proxy for representation compression, analogous to the role of 

I(X; Z) in the IB objective. To enable gradient-based optimization, we employ the ℓ1 norm of the mask as a convex surrogate for hard temporal sparsity. Therefore, minimizing ∥M ∥1 encourages compact explanations by limiting the temporal extent over which the input influences the detector, constraining representational capacity in a manner analogous to IB-style compression penalties. 

Task Relevance via Detector Response: In unsupervised anomaly detection, the ground-truth task label Y is latent and unavailable during training. Consequently, ARTA approximates the relevance term I(Z; Y ) by substituting it with the detector’s anomaly score AD ( ˜X). This score serves as a task-aligned signal that captures the detector’s internal representation of features that affect the anomaly score. By maximizing this response under sparsity constraints, analogous to minimizing I(X; Z),the generator identifies the minimal temporal regions sufficient to elicit an anomalous response. Thus, the −AD ( ˜X) term in the generator’s objective acts as a task-driven surrogate for mutual information, ensuring that the extracted Z is maximally informative regarding the detector’s decision-making process. Combining these elements yields the generator objective 

LG = −AD (X ⊙ M + (1 − M ) ⊙ B) + λ∥M ∥1, (14) which can be interpreted as a deterministic, task-driven approximation to the IB trade-off. Here, the sparsity multiplier λ

governs the balance between explanation compactness and detector activation, playing a role analogous to the IB trade-off parameter β. While this formulation does not provide information-theoretic guarantees, it offers a principled structural inter-pretation of the proposed objective as enforcing minimal, task-relevant temporal representations.

## C Baselines 

We evaluate our method against a diverse set of widely used time-series anomaly detection baselines, covering statistical, neural network-based, and foundation model-based approaches. These methods are selected to reflect different modeling assumptions, levels of temporal dependency modeling, and computational complexity. • PCA (Jolliffe [1986]): A classical linear method that detects anomalies based on reconstruction errors after projecting data onto a low-dimensional subspace. PCA serves as a strong statistical baseline for capturing global correlations in multivariate time series. • MCD (Rousseeuw and Driessen [1999]): A covariance-based detector that estimates robust mean and covariance statis-tics and measures deviation using Mahalanobis distance. This method represents distributional approaches relying on second-order statistics. • OCSVM (Sch¨ olkopf et al. [1999]): A one-class classification method that learns a boundary enclosing normal data in feature space. It is a commonly used baseline for unsupervised anomaly detection. • KNN (Ramaswamy et al. [2000]): A distance-based method that assigns anomaly scores based on the distance to nearest neighbors. It provides a simple non-parametric baseline without distributional assumptions. • LOF (Breunig et al. [2000]): A local density-based detector that identifies anomalies by comparing point-wise density to that of neighboring samples, capturing local irregularities. • KMeansAD (Yairi et al. [2001]): A clustering-based approach that computes anomaly scores as distances to assigned cluster centroids, representing prototype-based modeling of normal behavior. • CBLOF (He et al. [2003]): A cluster-based variant of LOF that measures anomaly scores using cluster assignments and inter-cluster distances, enabling scalable density-based detection. • IForest (Liu et al. [2008]): An ensemble-based method that isolates anomalies using random partitioning. It is included for its strong empirical performance and scalability. • HBOS (Goldstein and Dengel [2012]): A histogram-based method that assumes feature independence and computes anomaly scores based on bin densities. It provides a fast statistical baseline. • AutoEncoder (Sakurada and Yairi [2014]): A reconstruction-based neural network that learns compressed representa-tions of normal data. Anomalies are identified through elevated reconstruction error. • LSTMAD (Malhotra et al. [2015]): A recurrent neural network-based predictor that models temporal dependencies using LSTMs and detects anomalies via prediction errors. • DeepAnT (Munir et al. [2018]): A convolutional forecasting model that captures local temporal patterns through convo-lutional filters and flags anomalies based on deviation from predicted values. • Donut (Xu et al. [2018]): A variational autoencoder-based method that incorporates probabilistic modeling and missing-data handling, serving as a representative VAE-based baseline. ## D Hyperparameter Sensitivity 

In this section, we investigate the sensitivity of model performance to the two hyperparameters introduced by our framework: 

γ, which controls the emphasis on minimizing the anomaly score for perturbed inputs, and λ, which regulates the strength of the sparsity constraint imposed on the generator. To this end, we vary each hyperparameter independently and record the resulting performance. The results are reported in Table 4. For the robustness hyperparameter γ, setting it to a small value leads to a significant performance degradation. This setting effectively ignores adversarial perturbations in the detector loss, which contradicts the core premise of our method. Conversely, excessively large values of γ also harm performance, albeit to a lesser extent, since overemphasizing perturbed samples prevents the model from adequately learning the structure of clean, non-perturbed signals. A similar trend is observed for λ. When λ is set to a very small value, little sparsity constraint is imposed on the mask, allowing the generator to trivially corrupt the entire signal. As a result, the detector is trained on inputs that contain little to no meaningful information, leading to degraded performance. At the opposite extreme, excessively large values of λ enforce overly sparse masks, severely restricting the generator’s flexibility in selecting timestamps to perturb. In this case, the resulting perturbations become easy for the detector to identify, preventing it from developing robustness against worst-case, localized perturbations.

## F Comparison with Univariate FMs 

Motivated by the strong zero-shot performance of foundation models (FMs) in natural language and vision, recent studies have explored their extension to time-series data. Several such models have reported competitive zero-shot and fine-tuned results on downstream tasks, including anomaly detection. In this direction, models such as OFA, Lag-Llama, and Chronos are trained on large-scale time-series corpora with the objective of improving generalization beyond task-specific approaches. Nevertheless, while certain models such as OFA, whose multivariate results are reported in Table 1, support multivariate inputs, most existing time-series foundation models are restricted to the univariate setting. To ensure a fair and controlled com-parison, we therefore evaluate our method on univariate time-series datasets and benchmark it against both classical anomaly detection methods and recent foundation models. The results of this comparison are reported in Table 5. The datasets used are as follows: • NAB: The Numenta Anomaly Benchmark consists of a collection of labeled time series drawn from both real-world and synthetic sources, including server metrics, traffic data, and social media activity. Anomalies span point, contextual, and collective patterns, providing a heterogeneous evaluation setting. • Stock: This dataset contains high-frequency stock trading traces collected over a full trading day. Anomalies correspond to irregular trading behaviors and abrupt market fluctuations, resulting in highly non-stationary temporal dynamics. • MGAB: The Mackey–Glass Anomaly Benchmark is based on chaotic Mackey–Glass time series, where anomalies mani-fest as subtle deviations in chaotic dynamics. The dataset is particularly challenging due to the absence of visually obvious abnormal patterns. • IOPS: IOPS consists of performance indicators reflecting the health and operational status of large-scale web services and computing infrastructure. Anomalies capture service degradations and system-level faults observed in real production environments. • NEK: The NEK dataset is collected from real-world network equipment and includes multivariate telemetry reflecting network performance. Anomalies correspond to abnormal operating conditions and faults within network infrastructure. • UCR: The UCR anomaly datasets comprise a large collection of univariate time series spanning diverse domains such as physiology, astronomy, and environmental monitoring. Many anomalies are synthetically injected, enabling controlled evaluation across a wide range of signal characteristics. In addition to OFA, we consider the following foundation model-based baselines: • TimesFM Das et al. [2024]: A large-scale decoder-style attention model pretrained on a diverse corpus of real-world and synthetic time series using input patching. First, although LSTM-based models consistently achieve the strongest overall performance, the proposed framework remains effective across different architectural choices. Interestingly, transformer-based architectures do not provide any advantage over LSTM or CNN backbones. While attention-based models excel at capturing global context, their permutation-invariant formulation can overlook the local temporal conti-nuity required for precise anomaly localization. Second, Table 11 compares reconstruction-based and prediction-based detectors. Reconstruction-based detectors generally outperform prediction-based ones, especially under severe distribution shifts. This can be attributed to the fact that prediction-based models may implicitly smooth anomalies when forecasting over short horizons, whereas reconstruction error more di-rectly captures deviations induced by adversarial masking. Overall, these results suggest that while architectural choices influence absolute performance, the gains from adversarial training and learned masking are robust and not tied to a specific backbone or detector formulation. ˜X = X ⊙ M + (1 − M ) ⊙ B, 

where B represents a reference signal (e.g., per-sensor mean). To isolate the role of the baseline, we compare this formulation against a variant where the baseline is removed, i.e., masked regions are directly suppressed via ˜X = X ⊙ M .Table 12 reports the anomaly detection performance across multiple datasets. Incorporating a baseline consistently improves performance across all benchmarks. In contrast, removing the baseline leads to noticeable degradation, particularly on datasets with complex temporal structure. This behavior can be attributed to the fact that hard masking introduces unnatural distribution shifts and scale artifacts, which the detector may exploit as spurious cues. Baseline-aware perturbations preserve the overall signal structure and keep masked inputs on-manifold, enabling the adversarial training objective to more effectively encourage robustness and reliance on distributed temporal patterns rather than isolated point-wise deviations. 

J.1 Training Procedure 

ARTA is trained using a two-stage optimization strategy that combines reconstruction-based anomaly detection with adver-sarially learned temporal perturbations. The model consists of a detector Dθ , implemented as an LSTM autoencoder, and a generator Gϕ, which produces soft temporal masks that selectively suppress informative segments of the input time series. The generator and detector are optimized in an adversarial manner to jointly encourage robustness and information retention. We first perform a warm-up phase in which the detector is trained independently on clean input windows using a standard reconstruction loss. This stage stabilizes training by allowing the detector to learn a meaningful latent representation of normal temporal dynamics before being exposed to adversarial perturbations. Without this initialization, the generator may exploit trivial failure modes of an untrained detector. After warm-up, we enter a joint adversarial training phase . Given an input window x ∈ RT ×F , the generator outputs a soft mask m ∈ (0 , 1) T ×1 that indicates which temporal positions should be preserved. The masked input is constructed by replacing suppressed regions with a baseline signal equal to the temporal mean of the window, yielding an adversarially perturbed input ˜x. The generator is trained to maximize the detector’s reconstruction error on ˜x while enforcing sparsity on the mask, encouraging minimal yet maximally disruptive perturbations. Conversely, the detector is trained to minimize reconstruction error on both the original input and the adversarially masked input. This objective encourages robustness to worst-case, model-aware temporal perturbations while preserving sensitivity to genuine anomalies. The resulting min–max game yields a detector that is invariant to spurious temporal shortcuts and a generator that identifies temporally salient regions. At inference time, anomaly scores are computed from the detector’s reconstruction error, with optional variants that incorpo-rate the learned mask to quantify temporal sensitivity. The full training and inference procedure is summarized in Algorithm 1. 

## K Implementation Details 

Architecture. ARTA consists of a detector and a generator, both implemented using LSTM-based architectures. The detector is an LSTM autoencoder that operates on sliding windows of length T = 100 . The encoder is a single-layer LSTM that maps an input window x ∈ RT ×N to a latent representation of dimension d = 64 , using the final hidden state. The decoder is another single-layer LSTM with hidden dimension 64 , which reconstructs the input window by repeating the latent vector across the temporal dimension and applying a linear projection to recover the original feature dimension. Mean squared error (MSE) is used as the reconstruction loss. The generator is a single-layer LSTM with hidden dimension h = 64 . Given an input window, it produces a soft temporal mask of length T = 100 by applying a linear layer followed by a sigmoid activation to the final LSTM hidden state. The resulting mask lies in (0 , 1) T ×1 and is shared across all feature dimensions. 

Training Procedure. Training is performed using a two-stage strategy. In the warm-up phase, the detector is trained inde-pendently for 10 epochs using only clean input windows and MSE reconstruction loss. This phase initializes the detector to model normal temporal dynamics before adversarial interactions are introduced. In the joint training phase, which lasts for 100 epochs, the generator and detector are optimized alternately for each mini-batch with sparsity weight λ = 0 .01 , and robustness weight γ = 0 .1.

Optimization Details. Both the detector and generator are optimized using the Adam optimizer with learning rates 1 ×

10 −4 for each network. Training is performed using mini-batches of size 32 , with shuffled sliding windows constructed via a forecasting-style dataset wrapper. All experiments are conducted using a single GPU when available. 

Inference and Anomaly Scoring. At inference time, model parameters are fixed. Sliding windows of length T = 100 are passed through the detector to compute reconstruction errors. By default, anomaly scores are computed as the mean squared reconstruction error averaged over time and feature dimensions. In addition to this default detector-only score, we consider two alternative inference modes: (i) a mask-weighted reconstruction error, where reconstruction errors are weighted by the learned temporal mask, and (ii) a sensitivity gap score defined as the difference between reconstruction error on clean inputs and reconstruction error on adversarially masked inputs. Window-level scores are aligned with the original time series by padding the first T timestamps with the initial window score. 

Implementation. All models are implemented in PyTorch. Sliding windows are generated using a forecasting-style dataset interface with prediction length set to one.
