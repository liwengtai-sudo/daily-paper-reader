# CT-to-X-ray Distillation Under Tiny Paired Cohorts: An Evidence-Bounded Reproducible Pilot Study 

Bo Ma, Jinsong Wu, Weiqi Yan, Hongjiang Wei 

Abstract —Chest X-ray and computed tomography (CT) provide complementary views of thoracic disease, yet most computer-aided diagnosis models are trained and deployed within a single imaging modality. The concrete question studied here is narrower and deployment-oriented: on a patient-level paired chest cohort, can CT act as training-only supervision for a binary disease versus non-disease X-ray classifier without requiring CT at inference time? We study this setting as a cross-modality teacher–student distillation problem and use JDCNet as an executable pilot scaffold rather than as a validated superior architecture. On the original patient-level paired split from a public paired chest imaging cohort, a stripped-down plain cross-modal logit-KD control attains the highest mean result on the four-image validation subset (0.875 accuracy and 0.714 macro-F1), whereas the full module-augmented JDCNet variant remains at 0.750 accuracy and 0.429 macro-F1. To test whether that ranking is a split artifact, we additionally run eight patient-level Monte Carlo resamples with same-case comparisons, stronger mechanism controls based on attention transfer and feature hints, and imbalance-sensitive analyses. Under this resampled protocol, late fusion attains the highest mean accuracy (0.885), same-modality distillation attains the highest mean macro-F1 (0.554) and balanced accuracy (0.660), the plain cross-modal control drops to 0.500 mean balanced accuracy, and neither attention transfer nor feature hints recover a robust cross-modality advantage. The contribution of this study is therefore not a validated CT-to-X-ray architecture, but a reproducible and evidence-bounded pilot protocol that makes the exact task definition, failure modes, ranking instability, and the minimum requirements for future credible CT-to-X-ray transfer claims explicit. 

Index Terms —Medical image classification, cross-modality learning, knowledge distillation, chest X-ray, computed tomography.

1 INTRODUCTION 

# CHEST X-ray and computed tomography (CT) are two of the most widely used imaging modalities for thoracic disease screening and diagnosis. Chest X-ray is inexpensive, portable, and easy to deploy in routine workflows, whereas CT provides richer anatomical detail and often reveals disease patterns that are difficult to resolve on X-ray alone. In clinical practice, physicians frequently interpret these modalities together, but most computer-aided diagnosis systems are still trained and deployed on a single modality. 

1.1 Motivation 

Recent thoracic imaging models show that deep networks can learn useful disease representations from X-ray or CT alone [1], [2], [3]. However, single-modality training does not exploit the fact that CT can act as a richer supervisory source for an X-ray classifier when both modalities are available for related disease processes. Multi-modal fusion is one possible solution, but it often assumes simultaneous access to both modalities at inference time and therefore leads to more complex deployment. We instead study cross-modality knowledge transfer: can a CT teacher im-prove an X-ray student without requiring CT at test time? To answer this question, we formulate CT-to-X-ray supervi-sion as a teacher–student knowledge distillation problem. The teacher model operates on CT, the student model operates on

X-ray, and the distillation objective transfers class-discriminative information across modalities. This setting preserves a lightweight X-ray deployment path while still benefiting from information learned from CT during training. This problem remains under-defined in prior medical-imaging work. Same-modality distillation studies usually focus on com-pression within a single input domain, while multi-modal medical systems often assume that every modality remains available at inference. Cross-modality distillation is technically harder because the teacher and student do not observe the same appearance statistics, resolution cues, or failure modes. For that reason, even a negative or neutral result is scientifically useful when it is pro-duced under a transparent patient-level repeated-run protocol: it tells us whether the proposed transfer setting is currently supported by the data and implementation, rather than by optimistic narrative alone. This distinction matters beyond any single pilot study. If CT-to-X-ray transfer under tiny paired cohorts is not evaluated under explicit evidence constraints, it becomes too easy for the field to over-read small, unstable gains as method progress. We therefore study not only a concrete model configuration, but also a research setting that needs clearer evidential boundaries before stronger cross-modality claims can be treated as credible. The minimum decisive next experiment is therefore not another local tuning sweep, but a protocol that separates cross-modality supervision, same-modality teacher regularization, class imbalance, and mod-ule complexity on the same paired cases.

2 RELATED WORK 

2.1 Medical Image Classification on Chest X-ray and CT 

Deep learning has become a standard tool for thoracic image clas-sification. Broader chest-radiograph benchmarks such as the NIH ChestX-ray14 dataset / CheXNet and surveys of deep learning in medical image analysis show both the promise of large-scale X-ray recognition and the importance of dataset scale when interpreting reported gains [4], [5]. COVID-specific studies later showed that both chest X-ray and CT can support automated classification, but most of that work still trains and evaluates one modality at a time rather than studying training-only cross-modality transfer [1], [2], [3]. 

2.2 Transformers and Attention Mechanisms in Medical Imaging 

Transformer-based vision models [6] and hybrid CNN– Transformer structures have also been adopted in medical imag-ing. These approaches can model long-range dependencies more directly than standard CNNs, and they have been applied to tasks such as breast ultrasound analysis and CT classification [2], [7]. Hybrid designs that combine convolutional feature extraction with attention-based aggregation motivate the attention-inspired components of JDCNet, although our focus is on cross-modality transfer rather than pure backbone comparison. 

2.3 Knowledge Distillation in Medical Imaging 

Knowledge distillation transfers predictive structure from astronger teacher model to a lighter student model [8], [9]. In medical imaging, distillation has been used for abnormal chest X-ray classification, gastric X-ray learning, chest X-ray disease classification, and hyperspectral medical image analysis [10], [11], [12], [13]. These studies show that softened teacher predictions can improve compact models, but they mostly operate within a single modality. 

2.4 Privileged Information and Training-Only Auxiliary Modalities 

A second relevant line of work studies learning using privileged information, where additional signals are available during training but absent at inference [14], [15]. Closely related vision work on modality hallucination shows that side information can be exploited during training to improve a deployment-time single-modality model even when the auxiliary modality is missing at test time [16]. This framing is closer to the present deployment question than standard multimodal fusion because it treats the auxiliary modality as training-only supervision rather than as a permanent inference-time requirement. Our CT-to-X-ray setting can therefore be read as a privileged-modality medical-imaging problem: CT is potentially informative during training, but the deployed classifier must remain X-ray only. 

2.5 Cross-Modality Distillation and Representation Transfer 

Beyond medical imaging, supervision transfer across modalities has been explored through intermediate representation alignment between paired data sources [17] and through side-information set-tings that explicitly anticipate missing modalities at test time [16]. Related generic distillation mechanisms include hint-based feature transfer [9] and attention transfer [18]. These methods motivate the mechanism controls added in this study, but they were not devel-oped under the combination of severe modality shift, tiny patient-level paired cohorts, and deployment-realistic medical evaluation considered here. For that reason, the present paper uses them as controls for what should be tested next, not as proof that richer feature transfer is already justified. 

2.6 Evidence Robustness in Small Medical-Imaging Cohorts 

Clinical-impact reviews, COVID-19 prediction-model appraisals, and biomedical image-analysis benchmarking studies all empha-size the same warning: small or weakly described evaluation cohorts can produce optimistic rankings that do not survive closer scrutiny [19], [20], [21], [22], [23]. This concern is central to our setting because the paired cohort is much smaller than the all-X-ray cohort and because the initial held-out split contains only one negative validation case. We therefore treat evidence robustness not as a stylistic add-on, but as part of the scientific contribution: if a transfer claim changes materially under resampling, same-case controls, or imbalance-sensitive evaluation, then that instability is itself a result that needs to be reported. Taken together, the literature suggests a specific gap. Ex-isting medical distillation studies mostly address same-modality compression, privileged-information and modality-hallucination papers motivate training-only auxiliary signals without focusing on tiny paired thoracic cohorts, and evidence-robustness papers explain why such settings demand unusually cautious interpre-tation. In contrast, this work studies training-only CT supervi-sion for X-ray deployment under a reproducible, patient-level, evidence-bounded protocol. JDCNet should therefore be read as a hypothesis-driven pilot scaffold for testing CT-to-X-ray transfer claims, not as evidence that a richer module stack has already been validated. 3

3 METHODOLOGY 

3.1 Problem Formulation 

We study cross-modality thoracic image classification with two modalities, chest X-ray and CT. In the executable experiments below, the concrete label space is binary COVID-19 versus non-COVID classification, the evaluation unit is the patient rather than the image, and the deployment constraint is that only X-ray is available at test time. Let m ∈ { xray , ct } denote the modality, let x(m) denote an input image from modality m, and let y ∈{0, 1} denote the disease label. The objective is to learn an X-ray student network fS while leveraging supervisory information from a CT teacher network fT during training. This formulation should therefore be read as training-only CT supervision for X-ray deployment on patient-level paired cases, not as general inference-time multimodal fusion. 

3.2 Pilot Scaffold Overview 

Figure 1 summarizes the executable JDCNet scaffold used in this study. The teacher branch encodes CT only during training and provides class logits together with optional teacher-side feature refinement, while the student branch encodes X-ray and defines the deployed model. The scaffold exposes three optional mecha-nisms that can be tested rather than presumed: DPE as teacher-side saliency refinement, MHRA as teacher-side aggregation, and DFPN as student-side multi-scale fusion. 

3.3 Hypothesis-Driven Interpretive Scope 

The present method should be read as an implementation-faithful pilot scaffold rather than as a validated architecture. DPE, MHRA, and DFPN are mechanistically motivated, but the current evidence base is still too small to justify a strong conclusion that any one component is necessary. The fixed-split core comparison is centered on hard cross-entropy plus softened logit distillation, and the later resampling study adds attention-transfer and feature-hint baselines only as mechanism controls. Section 3 therefore exists to make the tested assumptions explicit and auditable, not to imply that the broader feature-alignment design space has already been covered or ruled out. 

3.4 Testable Hypotheses and Experimental Controls 

To keep the paper scientifically testable rather than architecture-descriptive, we map the proposed design to five explicit experi-mental questions. H1: a stripped-down CT-to-X-ray logit-transfer control can outperform student-only X-ray training on the paired cohort. H2: any observed signal is not reducible to same-modality KD alone. H3: any observed signal is not reducible to inference-time late fusion. H4: added DPE/MHRA/DFPN complexity im-proves upon the stripped-down cross-modality control. H5: any observed gain remains visible after imbalance-sensitive controls and threshold-behavior analysis. The experiments below are orga-nized to test these hypotheses directly, so that each architectural claim is tied to a corresponding empirical control rather than left as a standalone design narrative. 

3.5 Executable Module Summary 

DPE, MHRA, and DFPN are kept in the manuscript only to the extent needed for hypothesis traceability. In the current executable scaffold, DPE is a teacher-side spatial reweighting block that tests whether class-relevant CT regions should be emphasized before transfer; MHRA is a teacher-side attention-plus-retain-gate block that tests whether aggregated teacher interactions help more than direct feature passing; and DFPN is a student-side three-scale top-down fusion block that tests whether multi-scale X-ray fusion stabilizes prediction beyond plain logit KD. The source code and exported run artifacts document the exact executable operators, while the main paper focuses on whether these mechanisms change the evidence rather than on presenting them as standalone architectural contributions.

4.1 Datasets and Cohort Construction 

Table 2 summarizes the three manifests used in this study. The all-X-ray manifest contains 783 images from 424 patients and provides the most stable single-modality reference. The all-CT manifest is much smaller, with only 63 images from 25 patients, and is heavily imbalanced. For the cross-modality experiment, we build a paired X-ray-target subset with 26 X-ray images from 19 patients and match each X-ray to the closest available CT from the same patient using the reported temporal offset when available. The available cohort contains chest X-ray and CT only. All splits are generated at the patient level with an 80/20 train/-validation split. The paired X-ray-target validation set contains only four images (three positive and one negative), and the CT-only validation split contains only positive patients after patient-level partitioning. These numbers are sufficient for a proof-of-concept study, but not for a strong benchmark claim. That con-servative interpretation is already carried by the explicit support counts in Tables 2 and 3 and by the per-seed spread shown later in Figure 2. 

4.2 Experimental Protocol 

Teacher-only models use the larger teacher backbone, student-only models use the smaller student backbone, late fusion uses paired CT and X-ray inputs at training and inference, and distillation combines hard labels with teacher supervision. The current im-plementation uses lightweight CNN backbones, an input size of 

128 × 128 , AdamW with learning rate 3 × 10 −4, batch size 16, and five training epochs. We report two paired-cohort evaluation regimes together with two non-paired reference regimes. The first paired regime is the original fixed-split protocol, in which the paired cohort is evaluated across four random seeds (42– 45). In the revised paper, this fixed split is retained only as a feasibility screen because it preserves the original pilot setup but remains too small to support a strong ranking claim. The second paired regime is a repeated patient-level Monte Carlo protocol on the paired cohort, in which eight same-case resamples hold out five validation patients each (four positive and one negative), producing 5–10 validation images per resample (mean 6.9). This resampled protocol is the primary same-case evidence table for judging whether any apparent CT-to-X-ray transfer signal survives stronger controls. Because the original paired validation split contains only four images and one negative case, we do not treat bootstrap confidence intervals, calibration-error estimates, or paired significance tests such as McNemar’s test as numerically interpretable on that fixed split. Instead, the repeated patient-level resampling study serves as the primary uncertainty-aware control for whether the fixed-split ranking survives stronger same-case evaluation. We organize the experiments into three tiers. Tier 1: feasibil-ity controls tests whether any tentative transfer signal exists at all by comparing student-only X-ray, late fusion, same-modality KD, and stripped-down plain cross-modal logit KD. Tier 2: mecha-nism controls adds attention-transfer and feature-hint baselines to test whether generic intermediate-alignment losses are more informative than pure logit transfer. Tier 3: proposed-module test evaluates whether the JDCNet module stack improves upon those simpler controls. Temperature/alpha sweeps, module leave-one-out ablations, imbalance-sensitive controls, and progressive module-complexity experiments then function as stress tests on top of that core matrix. 4.3 Baselines 

The fixed-split headline table contains six configurations: one all-X-ray reference row plus five paired-cohort rows for student-only X-ray, late fusion, same-modality distillation, stripped-down plain cross-modal logit KD, and the current module-augmented cross-modality distillation variant. Teacher-only X-ray provides the strongest single-modality anchor on the largest available cohort. The CT-only teacher remains a data-availability reference only and is removed from the headline table because its validation split contains only positive cases and is therefore not a meaningful same-case comparator. For the repeated resampling study, the comparison set is delib-erately more question-driven. Feasibility controls ask whether any deployment-realistic paired-cohort signal is visible at all. Mech-anism controls ask whether generic attention- or feature-level alignment is more stable than plain logit transfer. The proposed-module test then asks whether DPE/MHRA/DFPN are adding value beyond those simpler alternatives. Under this framing, the resampled experiment is not designed to crown a winner; it is designed to show whether the fixed-split ranking survives stronger same-case evaluation. 

4.4 Fixed-Split Feasibility Screen 

The main observation from Table 4 is that the stripped-down plain cross-modal logit-KD baseline is the strongest mean paired-cohort result within this feasibility screen, while the current full module-augmented cross-modality variant still does not show a stable improvement. Under that conservative view, plain cross-modal logit KD reaches 0.875 mean accuracy and 0.714 mean macro-F1 across four seeds, exceeding the student-only baseline (0.813 accuracy and 0.571 macro-F1) as well as late fusion, same-modality distillation, and the current JDCNet variant, which all remain at 0.750 accuracy and 0.429 macro-F1. This fixed-split result changes the interpretation of the paper, but it still does not make the evidence strong. The plain baseline splits into two all-positive runs and two perfect runs on the four-image validation cohort, so its average advantage is driven by a tiny and unstable regime. The full-data X-ray teacher remains the most interpretable reference because it is trained on the largest cohort, whereas the CT-only teacher is kept only as a data-availability reference because its 12-image validation split contains no negative patient and is not a meaningful headline comparator. Figure 2 visualizes the same feasibility screen and highlights that the paired-cohort ranking remains unstable and data-limited. The main-paper interpretation is intentionally self-contained: support counts are explicit in Tables 2–4, and the per-seed spread is shown directly in Figure 2. 

4.5 Primary Same-Case Evidence under Repeated Re-sampling 

To reduce dependence on a single four-image validation split, we additionally ran eight patient-level Monte Carlo resamples on the paired cohort. Each resample held out five patients with one negative validation patient by construction, which yielded 5–10 validation images per split. Every compared model was evaluated on exactly the same held-out patients within each resample, so the resulting ranking is less sensitive to one accidental fixed split and more directly reflects same-case comparison fairness. Table 5 promotes the resampled summary into the main paper because it is the primary evidence table for the current prob-lem formulation. All paired-cohort comparisons in this table use identical held-out patients and identical label-balance accounting within each resample, so the remaining ranking differences are less entangled with split accident than in the fixed-split screen. The resampled ranking differs materially from the original fixed split. Late fusion now attains the highest mean accuracy (0.885), same-modality distillation attains the highest mean macro-F1 (0.554) and balanced accuracy (0.660), and student-only training remains competitive on balance-sensitive metrics (0.636 mean balanced accuracy). In contrast, the stripped-down plain cross-modal logit-KD baseline falls to 0.500 mean balanced accuracy with zero mean specificity, which indicates that its earlier apparent advantage does not survive repeated patient-level re-partitioning. The added mechanism controls do not reverse that conclusion: attention transfer reaches 0.562 mean balanced accuracy, feature hints revert to 0.500, and the full JDCNet stack remains below same-modality KD and student-only training on balanced accuracy and MCC. PR-AUC remains comparatively high across most rows because the resamples are still prevalence-heavy, which is precisely why specificity, MCC, and balanced accuracy are more diagnostic than ranking-style metrics alone in the current regime. These results do not prove that same-modality KD or late fusion are generally superior methods, because each resample is still tiny and contains only one negative validation patient. They do show, however, that the original plain cross-modal ranking is not stable enough to support a robust CT-to-X-ray transfer claim.

Because all reported runs already use inverse-frequency class weighting in the loss, we additionally tested a stronger imbalance-sensitive control by replacing shuffled sampling with a class-balanced sampler for three models: student-only, the plain cross-modal logit-KD control, and the full JDCNet variant. This in-tervention changed the picture asymmetrically. The student-only baseline improved from 0.625 to 0.750 mean balanced accuracy and reduced its mean positive prediction rate at threshold 0.5 from 0.938 to 0.625, indicating that part of its earlier error pattern was threshold- and prevalence-sensitive. In contrast, the plain cross-modal control dropped from 0.750 to 0.625 mean balanced accuracy and from 0.500 to 0.250 mean specificity under the same intervention, while the full JDCNet variant remained at 0.500 mean balanced accuracy. A companion threshold sweep showed that these models tend to collapse to all-positive predictions below 0.5 and all-negative predictions by 0.6, so the current score distri-butions still occupy a narrow threshold band rather than a robustly separated regime. Taken together, these controls reinforce that the present signal remains fragile rather than decisively separated. We also performed module ablations for the cross-modality pipeline by removing DPE, MHRA, or DFPN. The w/o DPE and w/o DFPN variants matched the baseline cross-modality result, whereas w/o MHRA increased mean accuracy to 0.813 and mean macro-F1 to 0.571. Even that improvement is still weaker than the stripped-down plain cross-modal logit-KD control, which now serves as the more informative reference for whether extra transfer machinery is warranted at all. Because the validation split contains only four images, these module-level differences should be interpreted as evidence of implementation instability rather than as definitive claims about individual components. Figure 5 summarizes these repeated-run comparisons. A progressive complexity experiment further clarifies where the present stack begins to lose value. Starting from plain cross-modal logit KD (0.875 mean accuracy, 0.714 mean macro-F1), adding DPE alone or DPE+DFPN reduces the mean paired-cohort result to 0.813 accuracy and 0.571 macro-F1, and adding DPE+MHRA or the full DPE+MHRA+DFPN stack drops it fur-ther to 0.750 accuracy and 0.429 macro-F1. This non-monotonic trend indicates that the current evidence does not support a claim that richer feature-level transfer machinery is helping under the present data regime; the simpler control remains the more defensi-ble reference even before one considers the added parameter cost. Table 6 collapses the current evidence back onto the five ex-perimental questions from Section 3.4 so that the claim boundary is explicit at a glance. Under the present data regime, H1 remains visible only as a tentative fixed-split observation, whereas H2– H5 are not supported once the stronger same-case, imbalance-sensitive, and complexity-aware controls are considered together. 

4.7 Implementation and Reproducibility 

All manifests, training scripts, repeated-run summaries, resam-pling studies, and exported figures are generated by a single executable pipeline. The revised pipeline now includes same-case patient-level resampling and generic mechanism controls in addition to the original fixed-split matrix, so the paper’s stronger uncertainty claims remain directly auditable. The paper is inten-tionally self-contained for review: support counts, regime roles, and headline conclusions are reported in the body rather than delegated to a supplementary PDF. We additionally provide an automated Kaggle download utility for auxiliary CT/MRI and public COVID imaging datasets, although the quantitative results reported here are restricted to the local chest cohort because the downloaded external sets are not same-patient chest X-ray/CT benchmarks.

current evidence still does not justify a robust CT-to-X-ray transfer claim under tiny paired cohorts . The fixed-split feasibility screen initially suggests that plain cross-modal logit KD may help, but the primary same-case evidence table shows that this ranking is not preserved once the held-out patients change. Second, balance-sensitive evaluation changes the story more than architectural detail does . 4.9 Limitations and Future Work 

The limitations of the present study are best understood in three groups. Data limitations: the paired cohort is very small, every resample still contains only one negative validation patient, and the CT subset remains highly imbalanced. Evaluation limitations: the present study still lacks an external cohort, the CT-only reference split is not clinically interpretable, and the resampling protocol im-proves uncertainty reporting without removing the underlying data scarcity. The next evaluation-focused step is therefore a better-matched held-out cohort, plus external testing once a sufficiently large paired set becomes available. The minimum decisive next experiment is now more concrete than in the original fixed-split version: a patient-level paired cohort large enough to support interpretable validation subsets, evaluated under the same same-case repeated-resampling protocol for at least student-only X-ray, late fusion, same-modality KD, plain cross-modal logit KD, a generic feature-alignment baseline, and the current JDCNet scaffold. Such a design would directly answer whether any future gain comes from cross-modality supervision itself, from architecture, or simply from data and class imbalance. More broadly, these requirements define not only the next step for JDCNet, but also a minimum evidential standard for future CT-to-X-ray transfer claims under patient-level paired evaluation. Accordingly, the present negative and fragile findings do not merely limit the scope of JDCNet; they also clarify the minimum evidence threshold that future CT-to-X-ray transfer studies should satisfy before stronger claims are made. 

5 CONCLUSION 

This paper studies cross-modality knowledge distillation between chest X-ray and CT for thoracic image classification under a deployment-realistic training-only supervision setting. JDCNet is presented as a reproducible pilot scaffold, not as a validated superior architecture. On the original four-image paired validation split, plain cross-modal logit KD attains the highest mean result, but that apparent advantage does not survive the stronger evidence checks introduced in this revision. Under eight patient-level Monte Carlo resamples, late fusion attains the highest mean accuracy, same-modality distillation attains the highest mean macro-F1 and balanced accuracy, and neither the plain cross-modal control nor the added attention-transfer, feature-hint, or module-augmented variants yields a stable cross-modality advantage. The main contribution is therefore a reproducible and evidence-bounded evaluation protocol for CT-to-X-ray transfer, together with an explicit record of which conclusions fail once same-case resampling and balance-sensitive analyses are applied. The present data regime is strong enough to rule out overly confident architectural claims, but still too weak to establish a robust CT-to-X-ray gain. That evidence boundary, rather than a new validated module stack, is the central result of this study.
