# ABSTRACT 

Photonic computing is a computing paradigm which have great potential to overcome the energy bottlenecks of electronic von Neumann architecture. Throughput and power consumption are fun-damental limitations of Complementary-metal-oxide-semiconductor (CMOS) chips, therefore con-volutional neural network (CNN) is revolutionising machine learning, computer vision and other image based applications. In this work, we propose and validate a fully photonic convolutional neural network (PCNN) that performs MNIST image classification entirely in the optical domain, achieving 94% test accuracy. Unlike existing architectures that rely on frequent in-between conversions from optical to electrical and back to optical (O/E/O), our system maintains coherent processing utilizing Mach-Zehnder interferometer (MZI) meshes, wavelength-division multiplexed (WDM) pooling, and microring resonator-based nonlinearities. The max pooling unit is fully implemented on silicon photonics, which does not require opto-electrical or electrical conversions. To overcome the chal-lenges of training physical phase shifter parameters, we introduce a hybrid training methodology deploying a mathematically exact differentiable digital twin for ex-situ backpropagation, followed by in-situ fine-tuning via Simultaneous Perturbation Stochastic Approximation (SPSA) algorithm. Our evaluation demonstrates significant robustness to thermal crosstalk (only 0.43% accuracy degradation at severe coupling) and achieves 100–242 × better energy efficiency than state-of-the-art electronic GPUs for single-image inference.

# 1 Introduction 

Convolutional neural networks (CNNs) have impacted various domains in artificial intelligence (AI) and machine learning (ML), such as image classification [ 8], computer vision [ 6], speech recognition[ 9], radio signal processing[ 10 ]and natural language processing[ 7]. Traditionally, CNNs faced many challenges concerning energy efficiency, latency, and scalability because they were implemented on electronic hardware like CPUs and GPUs [ 11 ]. These limitations have inspired the scientific community to look out for alternative computational paradigms like photonic and quantum computing[ 12 ][ 13 ][ 14 ]. Photonic Convolutional Neural Network (PCNNs) offers a breakthrough intersection of AI and photonics, showing huge prospects of solving some of the most fundamental challenges encountered in the electron based CNNs [15][16] .

The architecture of convolutional neural network (CNN), a type of neural network, generally involves convolutional layer, pooling layer and fully connected layer [ 18 , 19 ]. Convolutional layer apply convolutional kernels which performs the feature extraction. Feature selection and dimensionality reduction are performed by the pooling layer. At the end, fully connected layers make the final predictions by combining features using multiply-accumulate (MAC) operations. Among these layers, pooling layers are important to reduce the input dimension and overall model complexity [ 20 , 21 ]. The computational demands of modern convolutional neural networks (CNNs) have exponentially increased, exposing the fundamental energy and latency limits of electronic processors such as Graphics Processing Units (GPUs) and Tensor Processing Units (TPUs). Silicon photonics provides an alternative paradigm, enabling computation at the speed of light with extremely high bandwidth and low intrinsic energy dissipation. Previously, the PCNN architecture follows the hybrid method consists of both electronic and optical components, in which the electronic part performs the data storage, nonlinear activations and control units while the optical part performs the computation intensive tasks like matrix multiplications and convolution operation. [23, 24, 26] Most existing photonic neural network implementations focus on fully connected layers or require frequent conversions to the electrical domain to perform pooling and nonlinear activation functions. In this paper, we present a complete PCNN architecture that performs the all of its CNN operations—including convolution, max pooling, and activation— entirely in the optical domain. This became possible by three key advances in our PCNN model from hardware to algorithms: 1. Depth-Point seperable convolutional layer: Here we have designed the convolution layer which consists of two convolution operations. The first one is depthwise convolution, which reduces the input dimension, and the second one is pointwise convolution, which deepens the feature representation. 2. WDM based maxpooling: Here we have implemented a wavelength division multiplexing based maxpooling unit, which performs the maxpooling operations and reduces the image dimensions on different optical wavelengths. [17] 3. Pre-training ex-situ/ In-situ fine-tuning: Here we have pre-trained the PyTorch digital twin model with the exact number of parameters and trained it on GPU, transferred the phases to our PCNN model, and tested it on the test set. After testing, we used in-situ training for fine-tuning the parameters. While prior work has shown training of optical CNN on MNIST classification [ 17 ], but this is the first demonstration of efficiently training of PCNN, which includes on-chip optical nonlinearity, which was a major challenge. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first demonstration of fully integrated PCNN with hybrid in-situ training. Our PCNN architecture is expected to be the largest single integrated system till date, consisting 2,132 individually tunable parameters on chip, in terms of number of weights to compute the matrix multiplications and nonlinear operations coherently using optical fields. All the subsystems of coherent PCNN were combined, performing both linear and nonlinear operations, into a single 18 × 18 mm 2 chip. 

# 2 Architecture overview 

The PCNN maps standard CNN topology onto silicon photonic hardware. Instead of transistor-based arithmetic logic units (ALUs), the computation is executed through optical interference and passive delay networks. The architecture consists of the following key photonic building blocks: • Convolutional Layers: Implemented using rectangular Clements decomposition called Coherent Matrix Multiplication unit (CMXU) [ 2] which is a MZI mesh. For example, the first convolutional layer utilizes a 

10 × 10 Clements mesh containing 100 tunable phase parameters. • All-Optical Pooling: Pooling is achieved through a combination of multimode interferometers (MMIs), bistable disk resonators, and Ge 2Sb 2Te 5 (GST) phase-change attenuators, allowing max-pooling without electrical conversion [17] . • Wavelength-Division Multiplexing (WDM): Parallel processing is enabled in the secondary pooling layer via WDM, tracking 8 concurrent wavelength channels through shared delay networks and Arrayed Waveguide Gratings (AWGs). • Nonlinear Optical Function Unit (NOFU): Activation is provided by 32 high-Q microring resonators exhibiting carrier-injection nonlinearity[2] . • Fully Connected Layer: This layer contains majority of the parameters in which neurons of current layer is fully connected to the neurons of next layer. It is a combination of weighted Multi-mode Interferometer (MMI) and Mach Zender Interferometer(MZI). 2A PREPRINT 

In total, the architecture boasts 2,132 tunable thermo-optic phase shifter parameters mapping linearly to the network weights. 

# 3 Training approach 

A primary barrier in photonic AI is calculating gradients for on-chip training, as traditional backpropagation is physically unrealizable in photonic circuits. To address this, we developed a hybrid ex-situ/in-situ training pipeline. 

3.1 Differentiable Digital Twin 

We developed a PyTorch-based differentiable model that mathematically mimics the physical operations of every hardware component, encompassing optical insertion losses, asymmetric tap losses in delay lines, O/E/O scaling bounds, and NOFU clipping constraints. Backpropagation based pre-training is performed conventionally on a GPU. As we can see in the figure 1, with a batch size of 32 images and a learning rate of 0.001, the model quickly trained to surpass 85% accuracy and then asymptotised to 96.92%. Because the parity between the PyTorch twin and hardware simulation is numerically exact ( < 10 −15 absolute difference), the trained phase angles can be transferred to the hardware simulator with a 1-to-1 direct mapping, bypassing computationally expensive matrix decomposition techniques like Singular Value Decomposition (SVD).

3.2 In-Situ Fine-Tuning 

A straightforward hardware-based strategy for gradient estimation involves sequentially perturbing each model parameter 

Θ = [Θ 1, Θ2, . . . , ΘN ] and repeatedly passing the training data through the physical system [ 1]. However, a major limitation of this approach is its computational expense: for a model with N parameters, the training set must be processed through the hardware 2N times[2]. In contrast, our method updates all parameters Θ simultaneously. Rather than adjusting each parameter independently, the entire parameter vector is perturbed by the system along a randomly chosen direction ∆ in the parameter space, such that Θ → Θ + ∆ = Θ + [ δ1, δ 2, . . . , δ N ]. The directional derivative of the loss function during each iteration is evaluated as 

∇∆L(Θ) = L(Θ + ∆) − L(Θ − ∆)2|| ∆|| . (1) Following the principles of gradient descent, the parameter vector is then updated according to Θ → Θ − η∇∆L(Θ)∆,where η denotes the learning rate, a tunable hyperparameter. We have implemented an in-situ fine-tuning using Simultaneous Perturbation Stochastic Approximation (SPSA) algorithm. SPSA estimates the gradient of all 2,132 parameters using only two physical forward passes, naturally correcting for hardware noise and non-ideality such as thermal crosstalk between adjacent waveguides. # 4 Results 

Our implementation was validated on the standard MNIST handwritten digit classification dataset. We first pre-trained the backpropagation-based differentiable twin model. In pre-training, the model quickly trained to surpass 85% accuracy and then asymptoted to 96.92%. After pre-training, we transferred the phases to PCNN model and then tested on 10,000 test samples. With transferred weights, the PCNN model achieved 94.23% accuracy on ideal simulated hardware. To test structural robustness, we injected simulated thermal crosstalk up to a factor of 0.1. The dominant power consumers are the thermo-optic phase shifters (10.3 W) and the modulator drivers (3.0 W), yielding a total static power consumption of 14.7 W. # 6 Discussion 

6.1 Inference Latency Analysis 

A critical performance indicator for photonic neural networks is the inference latency τlatency , representing the interval from image input to classification output. In the PCNN architecture, τlatency is dominated by the serial streaming of image patches through the convolutional layers, rather than by optical propagation delay alone. The total latency decomposes as: 

τlatency = τConv1 + τPool1 + τConv2 + τPool2 + τOEO + τFC1 + τNOFU + τFC2 , (2) where τConv1 = P1 · τpatch ≈ 676 ns accounts for the P1 = 676 overlapping 3 × 3 patches streaming through the 10 × 10 

Clements mesh at τpatch ≈ 1 ns per patch. Subsequent stages are either pipelined with Conv1 or involve single-pass optical propagation ( ∼1 ns each), yielding a total estimated latency of τlatency ≈ 843 ns.

6.3 Thermal Crosstalk Robustness 

The PCNN architecture demonstrates remarkable innate robustness to thermal crosstalk, with accuracy degrading by only 0.6% under a crosstalk coupling factor of 0.12. We attribute this resilience to three structural properties: 1. The over-parameterized Clements meshes provide redundant degrees of freedom that absorb small perturbations without significantly changing the realized unitary transformation 2. The all-optical max-pooling operation inherently suppresses small-amplitude noise by selecting only the dominant spatial feature. 3. The NOFU microring nonlinearity acts as a soft threshold, attenuating weak crosstalk-induced signals below the activation level. 

6.4 Calibration and Digital Twin Fidelity 

The differentiable digital twin was calibrated against the hardware simulation accounting for beamsplitter imperfections, waveguide propagation losses, asymmetric delay line tap losses, O/E/O conversion scaling, and NOFU clipping constraints. Numerical parity between the twin and hardware was verified to < 10 −15 absolute difference at the individual MZI level, ensuring that phase angles trained via backpropagation transfer directly to hardware with a 1-to-1 parameter mapping—bypassing costly matrix decomposition procedures such as SVD. This strict parity is evidenced by the test accuracies obtained on the PyTorch twin and the hardware simulation prior to any in-situ correction, stayed within the difference of <5%. 

# 7 Conclusion 

We have demonstrated an all-optical convolutional neural network architecture capable of high-accuracy inference with hybrid differentiable ex-situ / SPSA in-situ training. The coherent PCNN model is robust against physical crosstalk perturbations, and our component power tracing proves its viability as low energy alternative to conventionally used deep learning accelerators. Furthermore a complete paper will be published with the full detailing of fully integrated coherent PCNN with each of its components and processes. In future, this simulation model will set paths for chip fabrication to assess the real world deployment performance. The pre-training was performed over the NVIDIA GPU RTX 3050 with 4 GB VRAM. The simulation of hardware testing with thermal crosstalk and fine-tuning was performed on an Intel i3 CPU with 16 GB of memory.
