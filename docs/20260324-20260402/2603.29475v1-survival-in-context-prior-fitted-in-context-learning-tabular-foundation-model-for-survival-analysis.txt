# Abstract 

Survival analysis is crucial for many medical ap-plications but remains challenging for modern ma-chine learning due to limited data, censoring, and the heterogeneity of tabular covariates. While the prior-fitted paradigm, which relies on pretraining models on large collections of synthetic datasets, has recently facilitated tabular foundation models for classification and regression, its suitability for time-to-event modeling remains unclear. We pro-pose a flexible survival data generation framework that defines a rich survival prior with explicit con-trol over covariates and time-event distributions. Building on this prior, we introduce Survival In-Context (SIC), a prior-fitted in-context learning model for survival analysis that is pretrained ex-clusively on synthetic data. SIC produces in-dividualized survival prediction in a single for-ward pass, requiring no task-specific training or hyperparameter tuning. Across a broad evalua-tion on real-world survival datasets, SIC achieves competitive or superior performance compared to classical and deep survival models, particularly in medium-sized data regimes, highlighting the promise of prior-fitted foundation models for sur-vival analysis. The code will be made available upon publication.

However, the target is often only partially observed: devices do not always fail, customers do not always leave, and poten-tial patients may drop out of follow-up in the observed time window. Such cases are censored: the event has not occurred by the end of recording, and it is unknown what happens af-terward. Treating time-to-event prediction naively as regres-sion (e.g., discarding censored samples) wastes information and introduces bias towards earlier events. Survival analysis uses both complete and censored outcomes, yielding risk estimates that evolve over time. This challenging problem is especially important for clinical decision-making, where models for disease manifestation (Seletkov et al., 2026), relapse (Zupan et al., 2000), and survival outcomes (Tesfay et al., 2021) must be able to model how a patient’s risk changes. In practice, survival analysis is most commonly performed on tabular data, particularly in healthcare, where each pa-tient is often represented by heterogeneous covariates span-ning demographics, measurements, and interventions. De-spite its prevalence, tabular data poses challenges that funda-mentally distinguish it from unstructured modalities such as text or images (Bommasani et al., 2021). In contrast to lan-guage modeling, where tokens share a consistent semantic meaning across datasets, a tabular value is a number whose meaning comes from its column name (and units), e.g., 120 for systolic blood pressure (mmHg) and temperature (◦C). This heterogeneity has historically favored classical task-specific survival approaches such as Cox Proportional Hazards (Cox, 1972) or Accelerated Failure Time mod-els (Prentice & Kalbfleisch, 1979). These problems have also long held back tabular founda-tion models in classification and regression (Hollmann et al., 2025), in contrast to the success of foundation models in lan-guage (Brown et al., 2020) and vision (Radford et al., 2021). Recent advances attempt to address this gap by introduc-ing the prior-fitted paradigm of TabPFN models (Hollmann et al., 2023; 2025). TabPFN is trained across millions of syn-thetic tabular datasets to act as a learning algorithm rather than a single-task predictor: given a new dataset, TabPFN uses in-context learning to infer dataset-specific structure 1

This helps it outperform strongly tuned baselines on tabular datasets while being orders of magnitude faster at deployment (Hollmann et al., 2025). This model produces predictions in a single forward pass and requires no hyperparameter tuning, unlike classical survival models. • We compile a diverse set of publicly available sur-vival datasets from previous work and major libraries to enable faithful comparisons across heterogeneous settings, evaluating against established baselines with extensive hyperparameter tuning. 

# 2. Related Work 

2.1. Tabular Foundation Models 

By leveraging multiple datasets and learning common rep-resentations, tabular foundation models promise to both im-prove performance and reduce implementation time through model reuse. A variety of transformer-based models tackle learning across tables by constructing joint representations from a varying number of inputs (Zhu et al., 2023), utilizing LLMs as tabular learners (Gardner et al., 2024), or adding the data semantics of columns to the input data in the context of clin-ical (Wang & Sun, 2022) or general data (Kim et al., 2025). However, these models require extensive pre-training and of-ten struggle to outperform tree-based baselines (Grinsztajn et al., 2022). The prior-fitted paradigm in TabPFN (M ¨uller et al., 2022; Hollmann et al., 2023) advances tabular foundation models by addressing these limitations through two key ingredients: pre-training on a large and expressive prior, namely synthe-sizing datasets that match the domain of interest, and in-context learning to approximate the Bayesian posterior pre-dictive distribution induced by this prior. Subsequent work has strengthened this paradigm by enhancing the prior gen-eration and architecture (Hollmann et al., 2025; Grinsztajn et al., 2025), refining the context selection strategy (Thomas et al., 2024; Koshil et al., 2024), and extending context size (Qu et al., 2025; Liu & Ye, 2025). The exhaustive eval-uation on the TabArena Benchmark (Erickson et al., 2025) demonstrates the effectiveness of the prior-fitted paradigm in classification and regression problems for small to medium-sized datasets. Our approach builds on previous work in the prior-fitted paradigm, introducing a new prior tailored to survival analy-sis. 

2.2. Survival Analysis 

Survival Analysis aims to estimate the distribution of event times in the presence of censoring. Methods can be catego-rized by whether they model time as continuous or discrete, which in turn determines the assumptions they impose on the hazard or survival function (see Section 3.1 for the formal definitions). To model time continuously, models impose structural as-sumptions between covariates and the event risk. Cox Pro-portional Hazards (CoxPH) model (Cox, 1972) is semi-parametric and assumes the hazard of one individual is proportional to the hazard of any other individual. When this assumption fails, the accelerated failure time (AFT) model (Prentice & Kalbfleisch, 1979) offers an alterna-tive. This assumes the basic shape of the survival func-tion could be stretched along the time axis to accelerate or decelerate the event occurrence. AFT models are also semi-parametric, as they do not specify the underlying dis-tribution in the time-to-event modelling. However, when domain knowledge suggests a particular prior, assuming the distribution of events can be advantageous, especially in limited data scenarios. Yet, this is strongly application-dependent, e.g., Weibull-CoxPH for gastric cancer (Zhu et al., 2011), Gompertz-CoxPH for aging mortality (Lenart & Missov, 2010), Log-normal-CoxPH for gallbladder can-cer (Wang et al., 2010), Log-logistic-CoxPH (Kumar et al., 2020) for lung cancer, Birnbaum-Saunders-CoxPH (Leao et al., 2017) for leukemia. Deep Extended Hazard (DeepEH) (Zhong et al., 2021) further unifies PH and AFT formulations under the extended hazard assump-tion (Etezadi-Amoli & Ciampi, 1987) as special cases that allows for more flexible survival characteristics. An alternative line of work models time discretely. Kaplan-Meier (KM) estimator (Kaplan & Meier, 1958) is non-parametric and makes no assumptions about the underlying event-time distribution or covariate effects, but only de-scribes a population-level survival and does not provide predictions for individual data points. The conditional KM estimator (Beran, 1981) addresses this limitation by ap-plying k-NN to incorporate covariates in survival function estimation. A kernel version of the conditional KM estima-tor (Chen, 2019) assigns weights to different training points according to a kernel function. This is further extended with deep learning in (Chen, 2020; 2023; 2024a). Another discrete-time method, DeepHit (Lee et al., 2018), directly estimates the distribution of events in discrete time, using a neural network trained with a likelihood loss and a ranking loss. While discrete-time methods are more flexible, they are known to be sensitive to the discretization grid choice. Another way to define the time-to-event problem is in terms of a neural ordinary differential equation model (Danks & Yau, 2022; Tang et al., 2022; Moon et al., 2022). Neural ODE models internalize time discretization and interpola-tion steps, reducing the need for manual time-grid design while retaining a continuous-time structure. In terms of neural net architecture by deep learning ap-proaches, the core survival methodology described above is typically implemented as a survival head, coupled with an encoder matched to the data modality, e.g., RNNs for time-varying data (Gupta et al., 2019), CNNs (Mikhael et al., 2023), or ViT (Erdur et al., 2025) for images, and transformers for longitudinal EHR data (Rao et al., 2026). To the best of our knowledge, no existing approach utilizes the prior-fitting on synthetic data and in-context learning for survival analysis. 2.3. Synthetic Data Generation for Survival Analysis 

Early work on survival data generation focuses on the statis-tical parametrization of survival times (Bender et al., 2005; Austin, 2012), assuming simple stochastic processes to gen-erate the covariates. Subsequent work introduces more ex-pressive latent-variable models, such as deep exponential families (Ranganath et al., 2016), later extended to broader distributional families (Miscouridou et al., 2018), while still focusing primarily on survival times. SurvivalGAN (Norcliffe et al., 2023) and the Ashhad frame-work (Ashhad & Henao, 2025) shift attention to generating covariates using conditional generative models. However, these approaches require conditioning on existing real-world datasets, which are scarce in the survival analysis domain. Our approach focuses on both generating covariates without relying on underlying real-world data and on modeling time-to-event outcomes by incorporating statistical properties and prior knowledge of common distributions encountered in Survival Analysis.

Survival in-context (SIC) model is trained solely on syn-thetic data. For the data synthesis, we first employ the Structural Causal Models (SCMs) (Pearl, 2009) proposed for data generation in TabPFNv1 (Hollmann et al., 2022) and enhanced in TabICL (Qu et al., 2025). 3.3. In-context Learning 

3.3.1. M ODEL ARCHITECTURE 

We employ the TabICL (Qu et al., 2025) model as the in-context learning architecture. The TabICL Encoder consists of the original column-wise inter-sample embedding and row-wise inter-feature interaction, producing 4 × [CLS] tokens hi per sample. To incorporate survival labels, we use a Time-event Embedding : the event ei is embedded via a linear layer applied to one-hot encoding, and the event time ti is embedded using a standard multilayer perceptron (MLP). We combine these embeddings mul-tiplicatively and add them to the sample representation: 

hi = hi + Linear (OneHot (ti)) ∗ M LP (ei). Finally, to produce the survival outputs, we employ DeepHit (Lee et al., 2018) as the Survival Head with the fixed quantile discretiza-tion of 10 bins (an in-depth investigation of the discretization is discussed in Section 4.5). We select the TabICL (Qu et al., 2025) architecture due to its favorable computational scaling and practical usabil-ity: for n samples and m features, TabICL has complexity 

O(m2n + n2) compared to O(m2n + n2m) of TabPFN ar-chitecture. This allows TabICL to perform inference up to 10 times faster and handle up to 500K samples and 500 features, compared to the TabPFNv2, which supports only 10K samples and 500 features. # 4. Experiments 

4.1. Datasets 

The evaluation of survival models is heterogeneous and remains insufficiently standardized. Analyzing previous work (Lee et al., 2018; Katzman et al., 2018; Nagpal et al., 2021; Zhong et al., 2021; Chen, 2024b; Wiegrebe et al., 2024; Knottenbelt et al., 2025) and the references therein, we compiled the datasets commonly used in the literature and those distributed with major survival analysis libraries, including pycox (Kvamme et al., 2019), scikit-surv (P ¨olsterl, 2020), and lifelines (Davidson-Pilon, 2019). To the best of our knowledge, the selected datasets encompass all pub-licly accessible clinical datasets in the field, comprising 13 datasets ranging from 137 to 25,000 samples and from 3 to 40 raw features. Additional details are in Appendix C. 

4.2. Baselines 

The proposed SIC method is compared against four differ-ent strategies. First, the CoxPH model (Cox, 1972) uses a linear model with a proportional hazards assumption. Deep-Surv (Katzman et al., 2018) applies a neural network to encode the non-linear dependencies under the proportional hazard assumption. DeepHit (Lee et al., 2018) models the survival distribution by discretizing the output times into a fixed number of segments using neural networks. XG-Boost (Chen & Guestrin, 2016) adapts the gradient boosting tree model for survival analysis. 

4.3. Evaluation 

Following the evaluation protocol of (Jeanselme et al., 2023), we employ nested 5-fold cross-validation, with 10% of each training set reserved for hyperparameter tuning. The reported results are the means and standard deviations across five test folds, computed using the time-dependent C-index (Ctd ). For the baseline methods, we use the Tree-Structured Parzen Estimator in Optuna (Akiba et al., 2019) with 100 trials for all models, setting Ctd as the optimization target. The hyperparameter search space is reported in Appendix D. The SIC model does not require any hyperparameter tun-ing and is evaluated using only a single forward pass. 4.4. Results 

Figure 3 presents the performance of SIC relative to the baselines in terms of the time-dependent C-index. Over-all, SIC demonstrates state-of-the-art performance across the majority of datasets. Across all 13 datasets, SIC is bet-ter than or on par with most dataset-specific baselines and is significantly outperformed by the best baseline on only two datasets: FRAMINGHAM by 0.023 and SYNTHETIC by 0.011 C-index points. It achieves statistically significant improvements on smaller datasets such as VETERAN com-pared to DeepHit and XGB, and on medium-sized datasets such as METABRIC compared to CoxPH and DeepSurv. The exact values are provided in Appendix F. To analyze performance across datasets with respect to dataset size, we compute per-dataset ranks for each method and summarize them in Figure 4. SIC achieves the best mean and median rank, suggesting strong generalizability across dataset sizes and survival tasks with only a single pre-trained model. Consequently, SIC shows stable performance across tasks, attaining rank 5 on only two datasets, com-pared to CoxPH, which shows higher variability, frequently attaining both top ranks and last-place ranks. Examining first-place rankings further reveals complementary strengths across dataset scales: SIC performs best predominantly on medium-sized datasets, DeepHit on larger datasets, and CoxPH on smaller datasets. ICL models avoid hyperparameter tuning and task-specific training by making predictions in a single forward pass, re-sulting in a speedup for the classification task (Hollmann et al., 2025; Qu et al., 2025). We confirm that these ef-ficiency gains also carry over to SIC in survival analysis. On the largest dataset, SYNTHETIC , SIC completes 5-fold cross-validation in 0.005h (18.3s), compared to 0.056h for XGBoost, 0.674h for CoxPH, 9.6h for DeepSurv, 19.9h for DeepHit, when performing hyperparameter tuning with 100 trials. This implies a substantial speed-up, making it a promising candidate for settings where fast, reliable, and well-tuned predictions are crucial, e.g., continually re-freshed pipelines in clinical operations or high-throughput hypothesis testing of many endpoints in biobanks. 

4.5. Ablations Sample size of synthetic datasets We evaluate the cur-riculum learning strategy as it is proposed by (Qu et al., 2025). The comparison of results across different stages is shown in Figure 5. Introducing a prior over 1,024 sam-ples in stages 2 and 3 improves performance on the largest datasets, SUPPORT and SYNTHETIC . Stages 2 and 3 per-form almost identically, indicating that extending the prior to 40,000 samples does not yield additional gains. This observation aligns with the findings of (Nagler, 2023): since the largest dataset in our evaluation contains 25,000 sam-ples, introducing a prior over 40,000 samples should not affect the results. In contrast, we observe a slight perfor-mance decrease on the smaller datasets, WHAS500 (500 samples) and AIDS (1,151 samples), compared to stage 1. Overall, the performance gains from curriculum learning are more modest than those reported for classification (Qu et al., 2025). Since this could be due to the number of samples available in our evaluation datasets and to ensure robustness on future unseen datasets, we used the model obtained after stage 3 for all subsequent evaluations. 

Generalization to larger data sets To assess how SIC generalizes beyond its prior definition at scale, we evaluate the model on the restricted-access datasets UNOS and SEER ,which contain 62,645 and 280,845 samples, respectively. The results are reported in Appendix F. On both datasets, SIC does not achieve the best performance, suggesting that extending the prior may be important when scaling to larger datasets. However, these extensions come with increased computational burden, including GPU memory limitations when training on larger datasets and longer training times.

We investigate the effect of the number of discretization bins (n bins) on the performance after stage 1. 4.6. Limitations and Outlook 

We do not consider calibration in our paper, although it can be important in practice. To improve calibration, the tuning of the α parameter in the DeepHit loss LDH , or an additional loss term, such as the X-CAL loss (Goldstein et al., 2020), or post-calibration (Mikhael et al., 2023), can be considered. A thorough investigation of the calibration properties of SIC is left for future work. The number of prior-fitted approaches for tabular learning continues to grow, along with the diversity of architectural modifications. We focus on TabICL as a representative state-of-the-art method due to its scalability and publicly available implementation. Although evaluating multiple prior-fitted models would broaden our comparison, such ab-lations require substantial computational resources. Future work may extend this analysis to alternative architectures,

How-ever, extending our model for recurrent events (Gupta et al., 2019), competing risks (Jeanselme et al., 2023), or time-varying effects (Kopper et al., 2022) would require not only changing the in-context learning architecture itself, but also introducing a new prior formulation that is able to capture these dependencies. 

# 5. Conclusion 

We introduce a Survival In-context Model (SIC), the first prior-fitted in-context learning model for survival analysis. Our approach is enabled by a novel method to synthesize survival data using structural causal models (SCMs) (Pearl, 2009) and the extended hazard assumption (Etezadi-Amoli & Ciampi, 1987). Together, these components allow the generation of covariates and time-to-event outcomes with-out relying on real-world datasets, while ensuring statistical properties. We extensively evaluate SIC on multiple sur-vival datasets and compare it against established baselines. Our single SIC model outperforms or achieves comparable performance to dataset-specific classical and deep learning baselines while requiring no hyperparameter tuning and be-ing orders of magnitude faster. This substantially simplifies deployment by eliminating the need to build a dedicated machine learning pipeline, reducing training overhead, and enabling prediction with a single inference pass. 8Survival In-Context: Prior-fitted In-context Learning Tabular Foundation Model for Survival Analysis

# C. Datasets 

The following datasets are used for evaluating the methods: • VETERAN Veterans’ Administration Lung Cancer. Randomized trial with advanced inoperable lung cancer treated with standard vs test chemotherapy, with baseline clinical covariates such as cell type and Karnofsky score. Target: Time to death from any cause. • LUNG North Central Cancer Treatment Group. Prospective cohort of patients with advanced lung cancer. Target: time from enrollment to death from any cause. • PBC Mayo Clinic Primary Biliary Cirrhosis. Cohort with primary biliary cirrhosis of the liver from a randomized trial D- penicillamine vs. placebo plus additional observational cases, including clinical and laboratory measurements at baseline (and, in some variants, longitudinally). Target: Time to death. Competing risk: liver transplant (not included). • WHAS500 Worcester Heart Attack Study. Patients hospitalized with acute myocardial infarction, with demographics and clinical variables such as age, sex, BMI, and comorbidities. Target: Time from hospital admission for myocardial infarction to death. 15 Survival In-Context: Prior-fitted In-context Learning Tabular Foundation Model for Survival Analysis 

• GBSG2 German Breast Cancer Study Group. Includes clinical covariates such as age, tumor grade/size, nodes, progesterone receptor, etc. Target: time to death from Breast Cancer. • AIDS AIDS Clinical Trials Group Study. Randomized clinical trial of HIV - infected patients comparing combination antiretroviral therapies, with baseline clinical and laboratory covariates. Target: Time to AIDS-defining event. • METABRIC Molecular Taxonomy of Breast Cancer International Consortium. Large breast cancer cohort with detailed clinical data and high - dimensional gene expression molecular profiles, used for integrative prognostic modeling. Target: Time from diagnosis to breast cancer death. • GBSG An extended version of the German Breast Cancer Study Group (GBSG2) with the Rotterdam Tumor Bank. The same features and target. • NWTCO National Wilms’ Tumor Cohort. The study includes patients with Wilms’ tumor from the 3rd and 4th National Wilms’ Tumor clinical trials, with information on histology (local vs. central), disease stage, age, etc. Target: Time from study entry to tumor relapse. • FRAMINGHAM Framingham Heart Study. Study focuses on cardiovascular risk, including traditional risk factors such as blood pressure, cholesterol, smoking, and diabetes. Target: Time from baseline exam to death from cardiovascular disease. Competing risk: death from other causes (not included). • FLCHAIN Assay of serum free light chain. The study examines the association between immunoglobulin light chain concentration and mortality, using serum free light chain (FLC) measurements with clinical data. Target: Time from blood sampling baseline to death from any cause. • SUPPORT Study to Understand Prognoses and Preferences for Outcomes and Risks of Treatment. Hospitalized seriously ill patients from multiple U.S. centers, with rich demographic, physiological, and diagnostic covariates, were studied to determine prognosis and end-of-life care. Target: Time from hospitalization to death. • SYNTHETIC Simulated survival dataset. Artificially generated survival data used for benchmarking, often designed with known hazard structure (e.g., non - linear or non -proportional hazards) and controlled censoring to test model behavior. Usually compared with clinical survival analysis datasets. • UNOS United Network for Organ Sharing derived cohort. Large transplant registry data, including recipient, donor, and clinical variables. Target: Time to death after heart transplantation. This dataset has restricted access 1.• SEER Surveillance, Epidemiology, and End Results Program. Population -based U.S. cancer registry covering multiple cancer sites with demographics, tumor characteristics, treatments, and vital status. Target: Time from breast cancer diagnosis to death. Competing risk: death from CVD (not included). This dataset has restricted access 2.
