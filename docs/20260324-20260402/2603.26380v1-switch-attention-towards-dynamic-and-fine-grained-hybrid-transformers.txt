Abstract 

The attention mechanism has been the core component in modern transformer architec-tures. However, the computation of standard full attention scales quadratically with the se-quence length, serving as a major bottleneck in long-context language modeling. Sliding window attention restricts the context length for better efficiency at the cost of narrower re-ceptive fields. While existing efforts attempt to take the benefits from both sides by building hy-brid models, they often resort to static, heuris-tically designed alternating patterns that limit efficient allocation of computation in various scenarios. In this paper, we propose Switch At-tention ( SwiAttn ), a novel hybrid transformer that enables dynamic and fine-grained routing between full attention and sliding window at-tention. For each token at each transformer layer, SwiAttn dynamically routes the compu-tation to either a full-attention branch for global information aggregation or a sliding-window branch for efficient local pattern matching. An adaptive regularization objective is designed to encourage the model towards efficiency. More-over, we adopt continual pretraining to opti-mize the model, transferring the full attention architecture to the hybrid one. Extensive exper-iments are conducted on twenty-three bench-mark datasets across both regular (4K) and long (32K) context lengths, demonstrating the effec-tiveness of the proposed method.

While they balance efficiency and performance of language modeling, most hybrid transformers adopt a static hybrid scheme (e.g. , alternating be-tween full attention and sliding window attention with a fixed ratio) designed heuristically prior to the pretraining stage (Brown et al., 2020; Jiang et al., 2023; Singh et al., 2025; Du et al., 2025). This limits the model’s potential to allocate computation more efficiently. For example, when the context be-yond the range of a sliding window is less relevant to the response that the model is about to gener-ate, one would expect a lower ratio of expensive standard full attention, and vice versa. Towards this end, this paper proposes a novel hy-brid attention mechanism named Switch Attention (SwiAttn ). As is shown in Figure 1, the proposed 

SwiAttn is a dynamic and fine-grained hybrid solution compared to prior efforts. In particular, 

SwiAttn inserts a router before each attention mod-ule and dynamically decides whether the input fea-ture should be processed by standard full attention or sliding window attention for each token at each transformer layer. The full attention branch gathers long-range information accurately from the entire sequence, while the sliding window branch mod-els short-term memory efficiently from a narrower context. The router dynamically decides the spe-cific branch for each token at each layer based on the input feature. In order to encourage the router towards the more efficient branch ( i.e. , the SWA branch), we adopt a regularization objective that is optimized with the log-likelihood loss. Addi-tionally, to efficiently transfer the knowledge from pretrained full attention models, we utilize contin-ual pretraining (CPT, Sun et al. 2020; Mendieta et al. 2023; Yıldız et al. 2024; Guo et al. 2025b) to convert the standard full attention architecture to the proposed SwiAttn . We perform extensive experiments on twenty-three benchmark datasets covering different context lengths against several competing baselines, and the results demonstrate the effectiveness of SwiAttn . The contributions of this paper are summarized as follows: 

❶ New Perspective : We present a dynamic and fine-grained perspective of hybrid transformer models for effective allocation of computation between full attention and its efficient variants. 

❷ Novel Methodology : We propose Switch Atten-tion ( SwiAttn ), consisting of both the full atten-tion branch and the SWA branch, to adaptively allocate computation for each token’s representa-tion at each transformer layer. Additionally, we design an adaptive regularization objective and optimize the model from standard transformers through continual pretraining. 

❸ Empirical Validation : We conduct extensive ex-periments on twenty-three benchmark datasets covering commonsense reasoning, in-context re-trieval, and long-context understanding. Further experiments reveal the dynamics of the routing, enabling fine-grained allocation of computation. 

2 Related Works 

2.1 Efficient Attention Mechanism 

The quadratic computational complexity has be-come a major bottleneck of efficient transformer-based LLMs as input sequences grow longer. This leads to a plethora of efforts in designing efficient attention mechanisms (Tay et al., 2022; Sun et al., 2025). One line of research attempts to reduce the computation of the standard full attention itself by sharing (Shazeer, 2019; Ainslie et al., 2023) and compressing (Ge et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024a,b,c) the key and value vectors, or optimizing hardware compatibility (Dao et al., 2022; Chang et al., 2025; Sanovar et al., 2025). Another line of work designs mechanisms to sparsify the standard full attention through heuristic (Zaheer et al., 2020; Xiao et al., 2023) or data-dependent patterns (Xu et al., 2025; Nawrot et al., 2025; Lai et al., 2025; Yuan et al., 2025). To circumvent the quadratic complexity, ef-forts are made in employing sub-quadratic attention (Yang et al., 2024; Han et al., 2024; Dao and Gu, 2024; Peng et al., 2025; Guo et al., 2025a; Kimi et al., 2025). However, their efficiency comes at the expense of degraded long-context performance, as their memory is limited by the size of the state ma-trices (Guo et al., 2025a; Nazari and Rusch, 2026; Sun et al., 2026). Conceivably, efficiency often comes at a price, and different mechanisms might be suitable for distinct scenarios, motivating the design of hybrid transformer models.

A common practice among existing designs is to interleave full and efficient attention layers ( e.g. , Figure 1(a)) according to a static ratio empirically defined before training (Wang et al., 2025; OpenAI, 2025; Xiao et al., 2026). Given the heuristic nature of these methods, efforts are made in automated search for optimal hybrid configu-rations under certain data distributions (Gu et al., 2025; Acun et al., 2025; Xia et al., 2026). Never-theless, most existing approaches adhere to a static hybrid scheme, either heuristically defined (Kitaev et al., 2020; Kimi et al., 2025; Yang et al., 2025) or searched with data (Gu et al., 2025; Liu and Wang, 2025), while recent works suggest distinct difficulty in generating tokens (Leviathan et al., 2023; Cai et al., 2024; Gloeckle et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024a; Fu et al., 2025). Therefore, this work proposes a dynamic and fine-grained hybrid trans-former to efficiently allocate computation across tokens and transformer layers.

4.3 Ablation Studies 

To investigate the effect of different mechanisms in the proposed method, we design several variants of the original SwiAttn . Specifically, V1 disables adaptive regularization and uses γbase instead. V2 removes the NLL term in Eq.8. V3, V4, and V5 changes α from 100 to 10 , 1, and 0, respectively. V6 and V7 perturb ε from 0.1 to 0.2 and 0.01 .We present results on three summarization datasets from LongBench-E in Table 4. 

Effect of Adaptive Regularization. From V1, V2, and V5, we can see that adaptive regularization, along with several factors in adaptive weights, is effective. The removal of adaptive regularization re-duces average performance by 1.3. Besides, when the NLL (V2) or MSE (V5) terms are removed, the performance also declines. 

Effects of Hyperparameters. V3 and V4 show the effect of the hyperparameter of α. When α is changed, the accuracy drops, as α balances the rel-ative importance of the MSE term in adaptive regu-larization. Variants V6 and V7 show the effect of ε.The model is less sensitive to this hyperparameter, and when ε is perturbed, the accuracy decreases slightly. ε controls the upper bound of the adaptive weights, which affects the optimization process.

5 Conclusion 

This paper presents Switch Attention ( SwiAttn ), a novel hybrid transformer that can dynamically route the hidden representation to either full atten-tion or sliding window attention at each transformer layer, enabling efficient and fine-grained allocation of computation based on the contexts. SwiAttn 

can be optimized via continual pretraining from off-the-shelf standard full attention transformers. An adaptive regularization objective is designed to enhance efficiency while maintaining accuracy. Ex-tensive experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed SwiAttn , and further empirical results reveal its dynamics and efficiency. 8Limitations 

One limitation of this work is the lack of a hardware-efficient implementation for computing the two branches during the prefill stage. Another limitation is that we have not verified the effective-ness of the method on larger models beyond 1.5Bparameters due to limited computational resources. In the future, we plan to develop hardware-efficient kernels and verify the method on larger models.

13 A Details about the Datasets 

In this section, we provide comprehensive descrip-tions of the datasets used in our experiments, or-ganized by the three evaluation categories: com-monsense reasoning, in-context retrieval, and long-context understanding. 

A.1 Commonsense Reasoning 

This category evaluates models’ ability to perform reasoning based on everyday world knowledge. • Lambada (LMB.) (Paperno et al., 2016): Aword prediction task requiring broad discourse context understanding. Models must predict the final word of a passage, which cannot be inferred from local context alone. The dataset contains 5,153 test passages extracted from BookCorpus. We report perplexity on the standard test set. • Wikitext (Wiki.) (Merity et al., 2016): A lan-guage modeling dataset derived from Wikipedia articles. We evaluate perplexity on the test split to assess generative capability over coherent, long-form text. • BoolQ (Clark et al., 2019): A binary question an-swering dataset where questions are naturally oc-curring yes/no queries from Google search. Eval-uation is based on exact match accuracy. • PIQA (Bisk et al., 2020): Physical Interaction QA, focusing on everyday physical reasoning. Models choose between two plausible solutions to a goal. Accuracy is reported on the test set. • SIQA (Sap et al., 2019): Social Interaction QA, assessing understanding of social commonsense. Models select the most appropriate response to a given situation among candidates. • HellaSwag (Hella.) (Zellers et al., 2019): A challenging dataset for commonsense natural lan-guage inference (NLI), requiring models to pre-dict the most plausible continuation of an event description among four candidates. Normalized accuracy is evaluated on the test split. • WinoGrande (Wino.) (Sakaguchi et al., 2021): A large-scale Winograd schema dataset with 44K problems designed to test pronoun disambigua-tion with adversarial filtering. Binary accuracy is reported. • ARC-easy & ARC-challenge (ARC-e/c) (Clark et al., 2018): The AI2 Reasoning Challenge, con-taining grade-school science questions. ARC-e consists of relatively straightforward questions, while ARC-c includes more challenging ones requiring multi-step reasoning. Accuracy is re-ported for ARC-e while normalized accuracy is reported for ARC-c. • OpenBookQA (OBQA) (Mihaylov et al., 2018): A dataset requiring external commonsense knowledge beyond the provided “open book” fact. Models answer multiple-choice science questions; evaluation is based on normalized ac-curacy. 

A.2 In-Context Retrieval 

Following Arora et al. (2024), this category as-sesses models’ ability to retrieve and utilize rele-vant information from a maximum of 4, 096 token context. We employ Cloze Completion Formatting prompts to align with next-word-prediction training objectives. • SWDE (Lockard et al., 2019): A structured extraction dataset where models answer ques-tions by retrieving attribute values from semi-structured web pages (HTML). • FDA (Arora et al., 2023): Food and Drug Ad-ministration document QA, requiring extraction of specific regulatory information from PDF doc-uments. • TriviaQA (TQA) (Joshi et al., 2017): Alarge-scale reading comprehension dataset with question-answer-evidence triples. • Natural Questions (NQ) (Kwiatkowski et al., 2019): Real user queries to Google Search with answers annotated on Wikipedia pages. • DROP (Dua et al., 2019): A dataset requiring discrete reasoning over numerical and temporal information. Models generate answers via span extraction, counting, or arithmetic. • SQuAD (SQD) (Rajpurkar et al., 2018): Stan-ford Question Answering Dataset, where answers are text spans from Wikipedia passages. 

A.3 Long-Context Understanding 

We adopt benchmark datasets from LongBench-E (Bai et al., 2024) to evaluate performance on tasks requiring processing of extended contexts. The benchmark is constructed from the following sources. • MultiFieldQA (MFQA) (Bai et al., 2024): MFQA is a multi-domain QA dataset with long 14 documents spanning legal, scientific, judicial, and government fields. Answers require synthe-sizing information across distant passages. • TRec (Li and Roth, 2002): TRec is a question classification task requiring models to categorize questions into 50 predefined topic classes. • MultiNews (MNs) (Fabbri et al., 2019): Multi-News is a multi-document summarization dataset containing news articles about the same event. • GovReport (GvR) (Huang et al., 2021): Gov-Report is a long-document summarization task based on U.S. government reports and congres-sional hearings. • Qasper (Qas) (Dasigi et al., 2021): Qasper is a QA dataset over NLP research papers, featuring questions requiring understanding of methodol-ogy, results, and claims. • TriviaQA (TQA) (Joshi et al., 2017): The Trivi-aQA dataset in LongBench-E is is a long-context variant of the original version, where relevant evidence is embedded within extended passages to test retrieval under length pressure. • SAMSum (SSM) (Gliwa et al., 2019): The SAM-Sum dataset consists of messenger-like conversa-tions as the input with summaries annotated by human as the target. 

A.4 Evaluation Metrics 

We adopt different evaluation metrics for these datasets. Specifically, for Lambada and Wikitext, we use perplexity. For BoolQ, PIQA, SIQA, Wino-Grande, and ARC-e, we use accuracy. For Hel-laSwag, ARC-c, and OpenBookQA, we use nor-malized accuracy. For in-context retrieval tasks, we use the “contains" metric and follow the prompt template of Arora et al. 2024. For LongBench-E, we use the default evaluation metric provided in Bai et al. 2024 with implementation from Gao et al. 2024. 

B Details about the Baselines 

In this part, we provide more details about the im-plementation of the baseline models. • SWA-ZS. The zero-shot sliding window atten-tion baseline is implemented using the corre-sponding full attention model. It directly restricts the attention span of full attention transformers to 1024 in alignment with SwiAttn .• SWA-CPT. The continually pretrained sliding window attention model is obtained by perform-ing CPT on the SWA-ZS model. We use the same amount of tokens as the proposed SwiAttn 

to train this baseline model. • StaticHybrid. The StaticHybrid baseline is im-plemented by alternating full attention layers and SWA layers. Specifically, we adopt the ratio of 1:3, where one full attention transformer layer is placed after three SWA transformer layers. In the 

22 -layer transformer model, the 4, 8, 12 , 16 , 20 -th layers are implemented with full attention, while the remaining layers are implemented with sliding window attention. The StaticHybrid base-line is initialized using the pretrained full atten-tion model and continually pretrained under the same token budget as SwiAttn for a fair compar-ison. • FullAttn. The full attention baseline is a 1.5Blanguage model with 22 transformer layers. The hidden size is set to 2048 . The standard version is trained on 240 B tokens of 4K context lengths, while the 32 K version is trained on 192 B tokens of 4K lengths and 24 B tokens of 32 K lengths. 

C Additional Implementation Details 

In the experiments, we adopt a 1.5B transformer model with 22 layers and the hidden dimension of 

2048 . For the attention module, group query atten-tion (Ainslie et al., 2023) is used with 16 attention heads and 8 key-value heads. The dimension of each query, key, and value vectors is set to 128 .In dual-branch routing, the router function R(·)

is applied after the root-mean-square (RMS) layer normalization (Zhang and Sennrich, 2019). For the 4K-length model, we first pretrain the full attention transformers with 240 B tokens, where we set the batch size to 1024 and train for 60000

For the continual pretraining of SwiAttn , we first initialize the model as described in Section 3.4 and then con-tinually pretrain the model for another 20 B tokens with the same batch size for 5000 steps. E Changes of Full Attention Ratio during Continual Pretraining 

In this part, we present the changes of the full atten-tion ratios during the continual pretraining stage. Specifically, we measure the frequency of the router choosing the full attention branch during CPT on the pretraining data. The averaged ratio of full at-tention is presented in Figure 9, while the averaged full attention ratio of each layer is visualized in Figure 8 (where the curves are smoothed for better visualization). From the results, we can see that the proposed SwiAttn chooses the full attention branch with a frequency of around 0.13 , which is more efficient compared to common static hybrid methods that alternate between full attention and SWA with a ratio of 1:3 ( i.e. , around 0.25 full at-tention ratio). Moreover, the per-layer statistics of SwiAttn suggests that different layers exhibit distinct behaviors: some layers ( e.g. , Layer 8, 11, 13, 14) tend to use full attention more frequently, whereas others ( e.g. , Layer 1, 2, 3, 22) tend to use SWA more frequently. Moreover, these results shows that the changes of full attention ratio is rel-atively smooth, and that the ratio stablizes after around 500 iterations. 17
